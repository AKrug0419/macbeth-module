Section 1 — Quoting from Your Primary Source

Your three story PDFs have stable, fixed page numbers — include a page number in every parenthetical citation from the primary source. The rules and examples below apply to all three stories: Hurston’s “Sweat,” Carter’s “The Company of Wolves,” and Roth’s “The Conversion of the Jews.”

Part One — Core Skills
1  —  Signal Phrases

A signal phrase introduces a quotation by naming the author or providing context. It typically includes a signal verb (such as writes, describes, explains, observes, notes, argues, suggests, reveals, or states) followed by the quotation. Avoid naming the author in every signal phrase; doing so becomes repetitive. Instead, alternate between signal phrases that include the author’s name and those that do not, placing the author’s last name inside the parenthetical citation when it is omitted from the signal phrase.

Two Correct Patterns — Alternate Between Them
With name in signal phrase: Hurston writes that “…” (page number).   |   Without name: The narrator observes that “…” (Hurston page number).
✓  Author Named in Signal Phrase

Hurston establishes Delia’s exhaustion and moral authority when she observes that the woman had “built it for her old days, and planted one by one the trees and flowers there” (4).

Author named in signal phrase → page number only in the citation.

✓  Author Named in Citation Instead

The girl’s composure in the face of the werewolf reveals a confidence that runs entirely counter to the helplessness the tale’s genre demands: she “stands and looks at him with her great, dark eyes” (Carter 9).

No author name in signal phrase → last name in the citation.

✓  Signal Phrase Follows the Quote

“You can’t talk to me like that” (Roth 4), Ozzie shouts at Rabbi Binder, revealing that his questioning has tipped over from curiosity into open confrontation.

The signal phrase can follow the quote as well as precede it — though these constructions require care.

2  —  Quotation Marks for Quotes

Enclose all directly quoted words in double quotation marks (“ ”). Commas and periods always go inside the closing quotation mark in American English unless a parenthetical citation follows, in which case the period goes after the citation. Use single quotation marks only for a quote within a quote.

3  —  Parenthetical Citations

After a quotation, place the author’s last name and page number (if available) in parentheses before the closing period. No comma separates the name from the number. The period ending your sentence falls after the closing parenthesis.

Format
“quotation” (LastName PageNumber).   →   e.g., (Hurston 2) or (Roth 6)
✗  Comma Between Name and Page Number

Delia’s voice hardens as she tells Sykes that “Ah hates you tuh de same degree dat Ah useter love yuh” (Hurston, 4).

Never place a comma between the author’s name and the page number.

✓  Correct

Delia’s voice hardens as she tells Sykes that “Ah hates you tuh de same degree dat Ah useter love yuh” (Hurston 4).

✓  Author Named in Signal Phrase

Roth opens the story with an image that signals the essay’s central conflict: “It was the last day of November, and the beginning of what Ozzie Freedman was to call the finest year of his life” (1).

Author named in signal phrase → page number only in citation.

4  —  Using Page Numbers — Stories in PDF Format

All three of your assigned stories were distributed as PDFs with fixed, stable page numbers. Include the page number in every parenthetical citation. Use only the number — not “p.” or “pg.” or “page.”

Format for PDF Sources
(LastName PageNumber)   →   e.g., (Hurston 3) or (Carter 7)
✗  Unnecessary “p.” Before Page Number

Sykes announces his contempt openly, telling Delia that “Ah don’t keer if you never git through” (Hurston p. 2) with the wash.

Never add “p.” or any label before the page number.

✓  Correct

Sykes announces his contempt openly, telling Delia that “Ah don’t keer if you never git through” (Hurston 2) with the wash.

✓  Author Named in Signal Phrase

Carter positions the girl’s fearlessness as the story’s central act of revision: “She will lay his fearful head on her lap and she will pick out the lice from his pelt” (10).

Author named in signal phrase → page number only in citation.

5  —  No Page Numbers — Stories in HTML Format

This section does not apply to the three stories assigned in this course, all of which were provided as PDFs with page numbers. However, some stories in academic databases appear as HTML webpages with no stable page numbers. In that case, omit the page number from the parenthetical citation entirely — include only the author’s last name, or omit the citation altogether if the author has already been named in the signal phrase.

Format for HTML / Web Sources
(LastName)  —  or omit the citation entirely if the author’s name appears in the signal phrase.
6  —  Stating the Author’s Name Only Once

The author’s last name should appear only once per cited sentence — either in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation, but never in both. If you name the author in your signal phrase, the parenthetical citation contains only the page number. This is one of the most common citation errors — and it applies equally to primary and secondary sources.

✗  Author Named Twice

Hurston shows that Delia has reached a breaking point, noting that “she lay awake, gazing upon the debris that cluttered their matrimonial trail” (Hurston 4).

“Hurston” already appears in the signal phrase — it must not be repeated in the citation.

✓  Name in Signal Phrase Only

Hurston shows that Delia has reached a breaking point, noting that “she lay awake, gazing upon the debris that cluttered their matrimonial trail” (4).

✓  Name in Citation Only

Ozzie’s rooftop demand strips away the adults’ authority in a single moment: “Tell me you believe me” (Roth 9), he shouts down to the crowd gathered below.

No author name in signal phrase → last name belongs in the citation.

Part Two — Common Quoting Errors
7  —  Isolated / Floating Quotes Common Error

A floating quote (also called a dropped or isolated quote) is a quotation that stands alone as its own sentence, without being introduced by a signal phrase or integrated into your own sentence. Every quotation must be attached to your own words to provide context and connection to your argument.

✗  Floating Quote

Delia has lost all feeling for her husband. “She knew that Sykes was planning a third wife” (Hurston 5). This shows how bad their marriage is.

The quotation stands alone as its own sentence — neither introduced nor integrated.

✓  Quote Integrated Into Student’s Sentence

By the story’s midpoint, Delia’s knowledge of Sykes’s plans has hardened into a cold, detached clarity: she understands that “Sykes was planning a third wife” (Hurston 5) and has already made her peace with what that means.

✓  Signal Phrase Introduces the Quote

Carter signals the girl’s transformation from victim to agent in the story’s final lines: “she is nobody’s meat” (10), a declaration that reframes the entire fairy-tale logic the story had appeared to be following.

8  —  Comma Splices Involving Quotes Common Error

A common quoting error is using a comma to join your own independent clause to a quotation. If your lead-in is a complete sentence, use a colon, not a comma. Alternatively, restructure the lead-in so the quotation flows naturally from it without needing a colon at all.

✗  Comma Between Complete Clause and Quote

Roth emphasizes Ozzie’s stubborn honesty, “he did not think it was an honest question” (2) when his mother asked if he was picking on the rabbi.

The lead-in is a complete sentence — a comma cannot join it to the quotation.

✓  Colon After Complete Lead-In

Roth emphasizes Ozzie’s stubborn honesty: “he did not think it was an honest question” (2) when his mother asked if he was picking on the rabbi.

✓  Restructure So Quote Flows From Incomplete Lead-In

Roth suggests Ozzie’s genuine confusion by noting that “he did not think it was an honest question” (2) when his mother asked if he was picking on the rabbi.

“By noting that” is incomplete — the quote flows naturally; no colon needed.

9  —  Colon Before a Quote — Signal Phrase Must Be a Complete Sentence Common Error

A colon may introduce a quotation only when the signal phrase is a complete sentence — that is, it has both a subject and a predicate and could stand alone. If your lead-in is an incomplete construction (such as “Hurston shows that” or “According to Carter”), do not use a colon; let the quotation flow directly from your words.

✗  Colon After Incomplete Lead-In

Hurston shows that: “de white folks had all but drowned her out” (3) when it came to her cries for help.

“Hurston shows that” is not a complete sentence — a colon cannot follow it.

✓  No Colon: Quote Flows From Incomplete Lead-In

Hurston shows that “de white folks had all but drowned her out” (3) when it came to her cries for help.

✓  Colon Correct After a Complete Sentence

Carter’s final image dismantles the genre’s foundational premise: “See! sweet and sound she sleeps in granny’s bed, between the paws of the tender wolf” (10).

That lead-in is a complete sentence — the colon is correct.

10  —  Punctuation Left Inside the Quotation Marks Common Error

When a quotation is followed by a parenthetical citation, do not leave the original text’s ending punctuation inside the closing quotation mark. Periods, commas, semicolons, and colons from the original text should be dropped — the citation and your own closing punctuation handle the end of the sentence. The only exceptions are question marks and exclamation marks that belong to the quoted material itself; these stay inside.

✗  Original Period Left Inside Quotation Marks

Delia’s defiance is finally audible when Hurston writes that “Delia’s habitual meekness seemed to slip from her shoulders like a blown scarf.” (3).

The period from the original text should not appear here — this also creates double punctuation.

✓  Original Period Removed

Delia’s defiance is finally audible when Hurston writes that “Delia’s habitual meekness seemed to slip from her shoulders like a blown scarf” (3).

✓  Exception: Question Marks & Exclamation Marks Stay Inside

Ozzie’s theological confusion surfaces in the question he cannot stop asking: “If he could make all that in six days, and He could pick the six days he wanted right out of nowhere, why couldn’t He let a woman have a baby without having intercourse?” (Roth 2).

Question marks from the original stay inside. The period after the citation still closes your sentence.

11  —  Missing End Punctuation After a Quote Common Error

When a quotation ends your sentence, the sentence still needs its own closing punctuation — a period, semicolon, or colon — placed after the parenthetical citation. The period after the citation is not optional.

✗  Missing Period After Citation

The snake’s arrival in the house signals the story’s climax, as Hurston describes “the huge snake” lying “in the lamp’s circle of light” (7)

The sentence ends after the closing parenthesis with no period.

✓  Correct — Period After Citation

The snake’s arrival in the house signals the story’s climax, as Hurston describes “the huge snake” lying “in the lamp’s circle of light” (7).

✓  Semicolon After Citation: Joining Two Clauses

Carter notes that the wolf-groom “would not hesitate to use his teeth” (5); this reminder of the creature’s danger frames the girl’s decision to stay as a choice, not a submission.

A semicolon after the citation is correct when joining two independent clauses.
