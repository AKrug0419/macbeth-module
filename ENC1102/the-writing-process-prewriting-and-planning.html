Stage One

Prewriting — Already Done

In the traditional writing process, prewriting is the work you do on a topic before you draft an essay — reading, viewing, analyzing, discussing, forming initial interpretations. For this essay, that stage is already complete.

In the Dystopian Literature & Film module, you studied the characteristics of dystopian societies, the types of dystopian control, and the traits of the dystopian protagonist. You applied those lenses to Minority Report during the in-class screening, and you wrote an analytical response about a film of your choice. That work is your prewriting.

This means you are already ahead: you have existing analysis, a working understanding of the dystopian framework, and practice identifying how dystopian elements function within a film. You can move directly into the planning stage.

Topic One

Children of Men

2006 • Alfonso Cuarón • Sci-Fi / Thriller

Analyze how two dystopian characteristics function within Children of Men to develop the film’s argument about what happens to a society that has lost its future. Cuarón’s near-future Britain is saturated with dystopian elements: militarized borders, government-issued suicide kits, caged refugees, propaganda broadcasts urging citizens to report immigrants, and a pervasive atmosphere in which human beings have been reduced to categories — legal or illegal, useful or disposable. Beneath it all, the protagonist Theo must navigate a world he stopped caring about long ago and rediscover a reason to act. Consider which two characteristics are most central to the film’s vision and how specific scenes make them visible.

Body Paragraph Focus
  • Body Paragraph One: Identify your first dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within specific scenes. Describe the scene in enough detail that a reader who has not seen the film can visualize it, then explain what the characteristic reveals about the society the film depicts. How does this element contribute to the film’s broader argument?
  • Body Paragraph Two: Identify your second dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within different scenes. Explain how this second element connects to the first — do they reinforce each other, create a cause-and-effect relationship, or reveal different dimensions of the same problem? How does the interplay between these two characteristics deepen the film’s critique?

Conclusion direction: What does Children of Men ultimately argue about the relationship between hope and social order? What does the film suggest happens to a society’s moral framework when the future disappears — and what does Theo’s arc reveal about what it takes to recover it?

Topic Two

Pan’s Labyrinth

2006 • Guillermo del Toro • Fantasy / Drama

Analyze how two dystopian characteristics function within Pan’s Labyrinth to develop the film’s argument about resistance, imagination, and the cost of authoritarian rule. Del Toro sets his story in the aftermath of the Spanish Civil War, where Captain Vidal’s military outpost operates as a miniature totalitarian state: speech is monitored, obedience is enforced through violence, dissent is met with torture, and ideology is treated as absolute truth. Meanwhile, Ofelia retreats into a mythical underworld where the rules are different but the stakes are equally lethal. Consider which two dystopian characteristics are most visible across the film’s two worlds — the historical and the fantastical — and how specific scenes in both registers make them concrete.

Body Paragraph Focus
  • Body Paragraph One: Identify your first dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within specific scenes — from either the historical world, the fantasy world, or both. Describe the scene in enough detail that a reader who has not seen the film can visualize it, then explain what the characteristic reveals about the kind of power the film is examining. How does this element contribute to the film’s broader argument?
  • Body Paragraph Two: Identify your second dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within different scenes. Explain how this second element connects to the first — do they mirror each other across the two worlds, escalate the film’s critique, or reveal something about the relationship between real-world authoritarianism and the structures of fairy tales? How does their interplay shape the film’s conclusion?

Conclusion direction: What does Pan’s Labyrinth ultimately argue about the relationship between authoritarianism and imagination? Is Ofelia’s fantasy world an escape from the dystopia or a form of resistance against it — and what does the film’s ending suggest about the cost of disobedience under totalitarian rule?

Topic Three

The Matrix

1999 • The Wachowskis • Sci-Fi / Action

Analyze how two dystopian characteristics function within The Matrix to develop the film’s argument about the nature of control, freedom, and what it costs to see the truth. The Wachowskis construct a dystopia operating on two levels simultaneously: the simulated reality of the Matrix, where billions of people live unaware that their world is a digital prison, and the ravaged physical world, where the remnants of humanity fight a war most of the species doesn’t know is happening. The film is dense with dystopian elements — total technological control, a protagonist whose awakening follows the classic pattern, surveillance systems that can inhabit any citizen, and an illusion of normality so complete that choosing to see through it requires an act of radical faith. Consider which two characteristics are most central to the film’s argument and how specific scenes make them operate.

Body Paragraph Focus
  • Body Paragraph One: Identify your first dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within specific scenes. Describe the scene in enough detail that a reader who has not seen the film can visualize it, then explain what the characteristic reveals about the system the film is critiquing. How does this element contribute to the film’s broader argument about reality, control, or freedom?
  • Body Paragraph Two: Identify your second dystopian characteristic and analyze how it operates within different scenes. Explain how this second element connects to the first — do they represent different dimensions of the same system, create a cause-and-effect relationship, or force the protagonist (and the audience) to confront uncomfortable truths? How does their interplay drive the film’s central question?

Conclusion direction: What does The Matrix ultimately argue about the relationship between comfort and freedom? Is the film suggesting that most people would choose the illusion if given the choice — and what does Neo’s journey reveal about the cost of waking up in a world designed to keep you asleep?

Stage Two

Planning — Body Paragraph Structure

The planning stage is where you decide what your essay will argue and how it will be organized — before you begin drafting. Even though you are not required to submit a formal outline for this essay, planning before you write is strongly recommended.

At approximately 700 words and four paragraphs (introduction + two body paragraphs + conclusion), the dystopian research essay is focused and tightly structured. There is no room for wandering — every sentence in every body paragraph needs to do specific work. The body paragraph structure below shows you exactly what that work is.

Writers who plan first tend to produce more focused, purposeful essays. If you’d like feedback on your outline before you draft, email it to the instructor.

Body Paragraph Structure

Every body paragraph in your research essay should follow this eight-part structure. During the planning stage, leave items ④ and ⑤ (the research source sections) blank — you haven’t conducted research yet. You’ll add them when you draft.

Body Paragraph Structure — Dystopian Research Essay
① Topic Sentence

Your own claim about which dystopian characteristic you are analyzing in this paragraph and how it functions within the film’s narrative. Your words only. No sources.

② Scene Description

Describe a specific scene from the film that illustrates this dystopian characteristic. Provide enough visual and narrative detail that a reader who hasn’t seen the film can picture the moment. Be concrete: name characters, describe what the camera shows, and identify what is happening in the scene.

③ Your Analysis

Explain what this scene reveals about the dystopian characteristic you identified. Films never explain themselves — your analysis connects what happens on screen to the dystopian concept and to the film’s broader argument. This is the heart of the paragraph.

④ Secondary Source

A quote, paraphrase, or summary from one of your secondary research sources — a piece of film criticism or dystopian scholarship that speaks to your analytical point. Introduce with a signal phrase. Complete During Drafting

⑤ Source Analysis

Explain how the scholar’s or critic’s point deepens your argument — not just that they agree, but specifically how and why their insight strengthens this paragraph’s claim. Complete During Drafting

⑥ Second Scene or Detail

A second scene, moment, or visual detail from the film that extends or complicates the paragraph’s argument. This can come from the same sequence or from a different part of the film.

⑦ Your Analysis

Explain what this additional evidence contributes to your argument. This analysis should advance the paragraph’s point beyond what ③ established — not repeat it.

⑧ Closing Sentence

Wrap up the paragraph’s argument and gesture toward the next paragraph’s topic (or toward the essay’s larger conclusion, if this is the final body paragraph). Your words only — no new evidence.

On Describing Film Scenes

In a poetry essay, you quote specific lines. In a film essay, you describe specific scenes. This means your “primary evidence” is not a quotation but a detailed scene description — what the camera shows, what characters do and say, what the audience sees and hears. The more concrete and specific your description, the stronger your analysis will be. Avoid vague summaries like “there is a lot of surveillance in the film.” Instead, describe the specific moment where surveillance operates and explain what it reveals.

Sample Outline — Minority Report
S
Minority Report
2002 • Steven Spielberg • Sci-Fi / Thriller
Sample Only
Thesis Statement

In Minority Report, Steven Spielberg uses the dystopian characteristics of technological surveillance and the illusion of utopia to argue that a system designed to eliminate crime can itself become the most dangerous form of control — not because it fails, but precisely because it works.

I
Introduction Paragraph

Introduce the film, its director, and its premise: Washington D.C. in 2054, where the PreCrime Division uses three psychic “precogs” to arrest murderers before they act. Identify the essay’s analytical focus: two dystopian characteristics — technological surveillance and the illusion of utopia — and how they work together to expose the hidden costs of a system that has achieved its stated goal. End with the thesis statement above.

II
Body Paragraph One — Technological Surveillance: Eyes Everywhere, Freedom Nowhere
① Topic
In Minority Report, Spielberg depicts technological surveillance as a system so total that citizens cannot move, shop, or exist in public space without being identified, tracked, and targeted — and the film’s most disturbing insight is that no one objects, because the surveillance is embedded in convenience.
② Scene
Describe: The personalized advertising scene in the shopping mall. Anderton walks through a Gap store and retinal scanners instantly identify him, greeting him by name and recommending products based on his purchase history. Every surface in the mall does the same — billboards, storefronts, and transit ads all call out to individual passersby by name. The sequence is shot casually, as though this is ordinary. (Spielberg, Minority Report, ~0:32)
③ Analysis
The retinal advertising scene illustrates how technological surveillance has been commercialized: the same eye-scanning infrastructure that enables PreCrime arrests also serves personalized ads. Spielberg blurs the line between state surveillance and corporate data harvesting, suggesting they are two faces of the same system. The scene’s casual tone is the point — citizens don’t resist because surveillance has been packaged as customer service. The characteristic of technological control becomes invisible precisely because it has been made useful.
④ Source
[Quote, paraphrase, or summary from one of your research sources — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑤ Source Analysis
[Explanation of how the scholar’s or critic’s point deepens this paragraph’s argument — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑥ Scene 2
Describe: The spider-bot apartment raid. To locate the fugitive Anderton, PreCrime deploys spider-bots — small, autonomous robots that swarm through an entire apartment building, scanning every resident’s retinas without consent. Families eating dinner, a couple in bed, children playing — all are interrupted and scanned. No one resists. The scene is shot to emphasize the invasion’s routine quality: this has happened before. (Spielberg, Minority Report, ~1:35)
⑦ Analysis
If the mall scene shows surveillance as convenience, the spider-bot scene shows its other face: total bodily intrusion. The robots do not knock; they do not ask. The residents’ compliance — their lack of surprise — reveals how deeply surveillance has been normalized. The body itself has become the site of state control: you cannot opt out of having eyes. Together, the two scenes argue that technological surveillance in Minority Report operates on a spectrum from seduction (personalized ads) to force (spider-bots), and that both ends of the spectrum depend on the same infrastructure.
⑧ Close
By presenting surveillance as simultaneously a consumer convenience and a tool of physical intrusion, Spielberg argues that the true danger of technological control is not that it oppresses people but that it makes oppression feel normal — a condition the film’s second dystopian characteristic, the illusion of utopia, depends on entirely.
III
Body Paragraph Two — The Illusion of Utopia: A Perfect System Built on a Hidden Crime
① Topic
The most unsettling aspect of the PreCrime system is not that it fails but that it succeeds — and Spielberg uses the dystopian characteristic of the illusion of utopia to argue that a society’s belief in its own perfection is precisely what makes it most dangerous.
② Scene
Describe: The Hall of Containment (“Hall of Souls”). Anderton escorts a visitor through the containment facility where PreCrime prisoners — people arrested for murders they have not yet committed — are stored in suspended animation inside translucent tubes, stacked in long rows. The prisoners are alive but unconscious, maintained indefinitely. A technician refers to them reverently as “the more drugged-out ones.” The facility is clean, orderly, and designed to look humane. (Spielberg, Minority Report, ~0:58)
③ Analysis
The Hall of Containment is where PreCrime’s utopian promise — the elimination of murder — reveals its hidden cost. The people stored in these tubes have not committed crimes; they have been arrested for what the system predicts they would do. The facility’s clinical aesthetics are the point: it is designed to look like a hospital, not a prison, because PreCrime depends on the public believing that pre-emptive justice is care, not punishment. The illusion of utopia operates through visual design — the system hides its violence behind clean surfaces.

[Quote, paraphrase, or summary from a second research source — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑤ Source Analysis
[Explanation of how the scholar’s or critic’s point deepens this paragraph’s argument — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑥ Scene 2
Describe: The revelation that Lamar Burgess murdered Anne Lively. In the film’s climax, Anderton discovers that Lamar Burgess — the director of PreCrime and Anderton’s mentor — murdered the precog Agatha’s mother years earlier to keep the program operational, then suppressed the “minority report” that recorded his crime. The system designed to prevent murder was founded on one. (Spielberg, Minority Report, ~1:52)
⑦ Analysis
Burgess’s murder is the film’s definitive statement about the illusion of utopia: the perfect crime-prevention system was built on the very crime it promised to eliminate. Spielberg’s argument is structural, not personal — Burgess is not a villain who corrupted a good system; the system required his crime to exist. The suppressed minority report proves that PreCrime was never infallible; it was maintained by concealing its own errors. The illusion of utopia is not a side effect of PreCrime — it is its operating principle.
⑧ Close
By revealing that PreCrime’s utopian success depends on a founding act of violence and the ongoing suppression of evidence, Spielberg argues that the illusion of perfection is the most dangerous form of control — because it is the one form citizens will never question.
IV
Conclusion Paragraph

Return to the essay’s central argument and explain its larger significance. Consider: What does Minority Report ultimately claim about the relationship between safety and freedom? If surveillance makes the system function and the illusion of utopia makes citizens accept it, what does the film suggest about the conditions under which people will tolerate — even celebrate — their own loss of autonomy? What does Anderton’s journey reveal about what it takes to see through a system you helped build?

Do not introduce new scene descriptions in the conclusion. Synthesize what your two body paragraphs argued and leave the reader with the essay’s broader claim about what Spielberg’s dystopian vision achieves.
