Week 9 • The Literary Research Essay

The Writing Process: Prewriting & Planning

Organizing your thinking before you draft your Literary Research Essay

✍️ The Writing Process

Stage One: Prewriting

In earlier modules this semester, you completed close-reading and analysis assignments on three short stories: Zora Neale Hurston's "Sweat," Angela Carter's "The Company of Wolves," and Philip Roth's "The Conversion of the Jews." In terms of the traditional writing process, those study question assignments functioned as your prewriting — the analytical work a writer does before drafting an essay.

Prewriting isn't a detour; it's how writers discover what they actually think. The close reading and analysis you practiced in those earlier assignments gave you the raw material you'll now shape into a research argument supported by secondary sources.

Stage Two: Planning & Organizing

The next stage is planning and organizing — deciding what your essay will argue and how it will be structured. For this stage, you'll create a formal outline before you begin drafting.

As with your first essay this semester, you'll plan and organize your body paragraphs before writing a single sentence of prose. Each of the three available topics below is built around the story you analyzed earlier, so your study question responses are a natural starting point as you plan your argument.

💡 Why Outline First?

Writers who outline before drafting produce more focused, organized essays — because the structural decisions have already been made. The draft becomes the place to develop ideas and find the right language, not to figure out the argument from scratch.

📋 What Your Outline Needs to Include

Your outline maps out the paragraph-by-paragraph structure of your essay before you begin drafting. Each Roman numeral represents one paragraph. Since your essay will be a minimum of 1,200 words, it will need 3–4 body paragraphs — and therefore 5–6 total paragraphs, including introduction and conclusion.

I. Introduction → II. Body ¶ 1 → III. Body ¶ 2 → IV. Body ¶ 3 → V. Body ¶ 4 (optional) → LAST. Conclusion

Here is what each section of the outline should contain:

I.
Introduction Paragraph

Identify the topic you selected — for example, "The Theme of Domestic Violence and Literary Symbolism in Hurston's 'Sweat.'" You don't need to write the full introduction in your outline, just name the topic clearly.

II–IV.
Body Paragraphs — Ten-Step Structure

Each body paragraph follows the same ten-step structure. Give each paragraph a descriptive title. Then for each step, write a brief phrase (not full sentences) describing what you plan to cover. For your primary source evidence steps, include the actual brief quote from the story you plan to use. For secondary source steps, simply leave a clearly labeled placeholder — you will find those sources later.

Body Paragraph Structure — Each of Your 3–4 Body Paragraphs
① Topic Sentence

Your own claim about this paragraph's aspect of the theme. No sources here.

② Primary Evidence

Brief quote from the story + brief phrase about what it shows

③ Your Analysis

Brief phrase — what the evidence means and how it supports your topic sentence

④ Possible Secondary Source

Placeholder only — leave space here. You will add the scholarly quote or paraphrase later.

⑤ Your Analysis

Brief phrase — how you'll connect the secondary source to your argument (to fill in later)

⑥ Primary Evidence

Second (or third) brief quote from the story + brief phrase about what it adds

⑦ Your Analysis

Brief phrase — what this second piece of evidence contributes to the paragraph

⑧ Possible Secondary Source

Placeholder only — leave space here for a second scholarly source if needed.

⑨ Your Analysis

Brief phrase — to be filled in when you add the second secondary source

⑩ Closing Sentence

Brief phrase — wraps up the paragraph and gestures toward the next. Your voice only.

LAST.
Conclusion Paragraph

You can leave this blank in your outline. The conclusion is easier to write after the body paragraphs are fully drafted, since it synthesizes arguments you've already made.

📌
Secondary Sources: Placeholders Only — No Research Required Yet

You have not yet learned how to find scholarly sources. Do not try to locate secondary sources for your outline. Simply leave a clearly labeled placeholder in each secondary source slot — something like "[Secondary source placeholder — to be added]". Over the full essay, you will eventually quote six times total from three scholarly articles. In a three-body-paragraph essay, that works out to about two secondary source placeholders per body paragraph. The sample outline shows exactly how to do this.

Topic One Zora Neale Hurston's "Sweat"
✓
Domestic Violence Literary Symbolism Religion Renewal

Discuss the theme of domestic violence as well as Hurston's use of literary symbolism in "Sweat."

First

Provide evidence of the emotional and physical abuse Delia endures throughout her marriage. What specific incidents reveal the full scope of Sykes's cruelty?

Next

Explain the symbolic functions of the rattlesnake: How does it symbolize Sykes, both biologically and in terms of the serpent's traditional symbolism within Christianity?

Finally

Discuss how the rattlesnake also symbolizes Delia's renewal and rebirth once she is finally able to escape her abusive relationship.

Topic Two Angela Carter's "The Company of Wolves"
✓
Gender Stereotypes Female Agency Masculinity Representation

Discuss the themes of gender stereotypes and representation in Perrault's "Little Red Riding Hood" and Carter's "The Company of Wolves."

First

How do the characters in Perrault's story conform to gender stereotypes? What image of women — and of men — does the original tale reinforce?

Next

What criticism about masculinity and male violence might Carter be making through her depiction of the werewolves? What female gender stereotypes does her version of the heroine not conform to?

Finally

By using a combination of intelligence and sexuality to save herself, what might Carter be suggesting about how women's intelligence and sexuality are frequently portrayed — especially by men?

Topic Three Philip Roth's "The Conversion of the Jews"
✓
Intellectual Intolerance Faith & Doubt Authority

Discuss the theme of intellectual intolerance in Philip Roth's "The Conversion of the Jews."

First

What specific tenets of Jewish theology is Ozzie confused about — and therefore questions? What is the actual logical structure of his argument?

Next

How do his mother and Rabbi Binder both demonstrate intolerance toward Ozzie and his questions? What forms does that intolerance take?

Finally

Discuss the thematic significance of the rooftop climax: Though Ozzie isn't literally converting anyone to Christianity, he wants his mother and Rabbi Binder to change. What does he want them to change about themselves by forcing them to "believe that God can do anything"?

📄 Sample Outline

Review the Sample Before You Start

A sample research essay outline is available for you to review before completing your own. The sample is based on Angela Carter's "The Tiger's Bride" — a story you are not writing about — and focuses on the theme of the male gaze and female self-transformation. It models the exact structure your own outline should follow: the ten-step body paragraph, the use of brief phrases and actual story quotes as supporting details, and clearly marked placeholders where secondary sources will eventually go.

The sample shows you how to plan your argument before you have your secondary sources in hand. You will notice that every secondary source slot in the sample is a placeholder, not a filled-in quote. Your outline should do the same thing. Review the sample carefully before beginning your own outline.

🔖 This Is a Research Essay Outline — But You Don't Need Sources Yet

Your final essay will incorporate both primary sources (quotes from the story) and secondary sources (quotes from scholarly articles). For your outline, you only need to include the primary source quotes and leave labeled placeholders for secondary sources. You haven't yet learned how to find scholarly articles — that research stage comes later.

✅ Before You Start Writing

Make sure you can check off each of the following before opening the Outline Assignment:

  • You've chosen your topic (Topic One, Two, or Three)
  • You know which short story you're writing about
  • You've reviewed the sample outline
  • You know the general focus of each body paragraph
  • Each body paragraph has a distinct aspect of the theme to analyze
  • You've identified which of your study question responses connect to your chosen topic
  • You're ready to open the Outline Assignment
🗂️ Using Your Study Questions as Prewriting

Your study question responses from earlier in the course are the best starting point for your outline. Look back at your answers for the story you've chosen. The evidence you gathered and the analysis you wrote in those responses is the raw material for your body paragraph sub-points. You don't need to start from scratch — you're building on work you've already done.
