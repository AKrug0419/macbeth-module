Writing about Film

The rules for treating a film as a primary source: how to format titles, what tense to write in, how to handle dialogue, and how to cite specific moments using the director plus timecode.

A film, for the purposes of MLA documentation, is treated much the way a short story or a novel is treated: it is a primary source, and you refer to it constantly throughout your essay. The rules below are the ones you will use on every page of your draft. They are not optional stylistic choices. They are the conventions that tell a reader of your essay that you are writing an analysis, not a review or a plot summary.

SEC 01

The Core Rules

Rule 1

Italicize Film Titles Every Time

Film titles are always italicized, both in your prose and in your Works Cited entry. This is the same rule you already know for novels and long works. Shorter titles (a song, an episode of a TV series, a short story) go in quotation marks; a full feature film is italicized.

Do not underline. Do not put the title in quotation marks. Do not bold it. Italicize it, and do it consistently from the first sentence of your introduction to the last line of your conclusion.

✗ Incorrect

In "Minority Report," Spielberg imagines a near future shaped by surveillance.

✓ Correct

In Minority Report, Spielberg imagines a near future shaped by surveillance.

Note: when a scholar writes about the film, the title of their article is in quotation marks and the film name inside the article title stays italicized: Bakewell's "The One-Eyed Man Is King: Oedipal Vision in Minority Report."

Rule 2

Write in the Present Tense (the Literary Present)

When you describe what happens in a film, you write in the present tense, not the past. This is the same convention you would use writing about a novel or a play. It is called the literary present, and it reflects the fact that the events of a work of art are always happening, every time the work is read or watched.

Choose the present tense and stick with it. Slipping into past tense for plot summary is one of the most common problems in student drafts of film papers, and it almost always creeps in during descriptions of action sequences, where past tense feels natural because the action unfolded over time.

✗ Incorrect (past tense)

Anderton ran from the spider drones and hid in the ice bath to avoid having his retinas scanned. Spielberg shot the scene in cool blue tones to emphasize the horror of total surveillance.

✓ Correct (present tense)

Anderton runs from the spider drones and hides in the ice bath to avoid having his retinas scanned. Spielberg shoots the scene in cool blue tones to emphasize the horror of total surveillance.

The same rule applies to the filmmakers themselves. Spielberg directs, not directed. The film argues, not argued. A scholar points out, not pointed out. Verbs that describe what a text or its maker does are present tense by convention, even though the film was made decades ago.

Rule 3

Dialogue Goes in Quotation Marks

When you quote spoken dialogue from a film, treat it exactly the way you would treat a line of dialogue from a novel: put it inside double quotation marks. Do not italicize dialogue. Do not set it off as a block quote unless it runs longer than four lines of your own typed prose, which is rare in film writing.

If the line you are quoting contains a word or phrase that is itself in quotation marks inside the film's script, convert those inner quotation marks to single quotation marks.

✓ Correct

Iris Hineman, speaking from inside a greenhouse full of carnivorous plants, tells Anderton that "in the land of the blind, the one eyed man is king" (Spielberg 01:14:22).

Notice that the quoted line is punctuated as part of your own sentence. The period comes after the parenthetical citation, not inside the quotation marks. See Rule 4 for the details of the parenthetical itself.

Rule 4

Parenthetical Citation: Director's Last Name and Timecode

When you cite a specific moment in the film, whether you are quoting dialogue or describing a shot, the parenthetical citation follows a single format: director's last name, a space, and the timecode in hh:mm:ss format. If the moment spans more than a few seconds, give the start and end times separated by the word to.

✓ Single moment

Anderton reaches into the shimmering holographic image of his son, and his hand passes through the light (Spielberg 00:22:14).

✓ Range of time

A swarm of mechanical spider drones crawls under doors and climbs onto sleeping faces to scan each resident's retina in turn (Spielberg 01:09:30 to 01:11:00).

If you have already named the director in your signal phrase, you may drop his name from the parenthetical and give just the timecode.

✓ Director in signal phrase

Spielberg opens the film with the Precrime temple sequence, three pale figures floating in a shallow pool as their visions are projected onto curved glass (00:04:18 to 00:06:42).

Use the word to, not a hyphen or a dash, between start and end timecodes. MLA 9 prefers the written form for clarity.

Rule 5

First Reference vs. Later References

The first time you mention the film in your essay, give both the title (italicized) and the director's full name. After that, you can refer to the film by its title alone, or to the director by last name alone.

✓ First reference

Steven Spielberg's Minority Report (2002) opens inside a circular chamber that the officers of Precrime literally call "the temple."

✓ Later references

Throughout Minority Report, Spielberg returns to the same visual motif: an eye that is being watched, scanned, or surgically replaced.

You may also include the release year in parentheses on first reference, as shown above. This is optional in a short essay, but it is a clean habit to develop.

Rule 6

Refer to Characters by Character Name, Not Actor Name

When you describe what happens in the film, refer to the people on screen by their character names, not by the actors playing them. Minority Report stars Tom Cruise, but in your essay, the protagonist is John Anderton, not Tom Cruise. Use actor names only when you are specifically making a point about the performance or the casting.

✗ Incorrect

Tom Cruise runs across the rooftops as he tries to outpace his former colleagues. Colin Farrell pursues him with calm determination.

✓ Correct

Anderton runs across the rooftops as he tries to outpace his former colleagues. Detective Danny Witwer pursues him with calm determination.

SEC 02

Quick Reference

Keep this next to you while you draft. Every rule on this page is condensed into a single row.

Writing about Film • At a Glance
Film Title
Italicized every time: Minority Report. Not in quotation marks, not underlined.
Verb Tense
Present tense. The film opens, the camera pans, Spielberg argues. Never "the film opened" or "Spielberg argued."
Dialogue
Double quotation marks. Inner quotations become single quotation marks. Periods go outside the quotation marks when a parenthetical follows.
Parenthetical
Director last name plus timecode in hh:mm:ss: (Spielberg 01:14:22). For ranges: (Spielberg 00:04:18 to 00:06:42).
If Name in Signal
Drop the director from the parenthetical, keep only the timecode: (01:14:22).
First Reference
Give full director name plus italicized title: Steven Spielberg's Minority Report. After that, title or last name alone is fine.
Characters
Refer to people by their character names, not the actors. Anderton, not Tom Cruise.
Works Cited
One entry for the film, beginning with the italicized title: Minority Report. Directed by Steven Spielberg, Twentieth Century Fox and DreamWorks Pictures, 2002.
Remember

These rules apply to the primary source only: the film itself. Citing your secondary sources (scholarly articles and web reviews) follows a different set of rules, since those sources have authors, page numbers (sometimes), and publication venues. For secondary source citation, see MLA Documentation: Quoting from Your Secondary Research Sources.
