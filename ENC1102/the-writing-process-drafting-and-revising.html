SEC · 01

Drafting Your Essay

You have done the hard thinking. You have a film, an angle, a thesis, an outline, and your sources gathered. Drafting is the stage where you translate that preparation into continuous prose, paragraphs that flow from one to the next and build a single, coherent argument. It is worth remembering that a first draft is not a finished essay. It is a working document. Your primary goal in drafting is to get the ideas down in the right order. Worrying too much about sentence level perfection at this stage slows you down. Revision is what the next pass is for.

For this essay, your drafting stage includes one task you deferred from your outline: filling in the secondary source sections of each body paragraph. This is the stage where you return to your three sources and decide exactly how to integrate each one at the most effective point in your argument.

Three Required Types of Source Integration

Your three secondary sources must appear as three different types of integration: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary. Review the MLA Documentation pages from this module, especially the How to Write a Summary page, if you need a refresher on how to write a summary or paraphrase, or how to format parenthetical citations for PDF vs. HTML sources.

Citing the Film: Timestamps

When you quote dialogue from your film or describe a specific shot, MLA 9 requires timestamps in the parenthetical citation, not page numbers. Use the format (hours:minutes:seconds) for the start of the moment, with an en range only if you are citing a longer passage. For example, a single line of dialogue might appear as (Spielberg 01:14:22), while a longer scene might appear as (Spielberg 01:14:22 to 01:15:08). Use the word "to" between timestamps rather than a dash. The film itself is your primary source and goes on the Works Cited page.

The essay below models the structure, source integration, and argumentative approach your own essay should follow. It is written about Steven Spielberg's Minority Report (2002), a film that is not on the approved list for your assignment, so that you can see exactly how the moves work without anyone being tempted to lift sentences. In the Country of the Blind: Eyes, Surveillance, and the Cost of Perfect Sight in Steven Spielberg's Minority Report

• Introduction
① Creative Opening
Opens with a verifiable historical fact: a 16th century proverb collected by Erasmus that the film itself quotes.
② Film and Director Introduced
Title, director, year, and source story identified; historical context established.
③ Thesis Statement
Last sentence; states exactly what the two image registers do and what argument they support.

In 1500, the Dutch humanist Desiderius Erasmus published a collection of classical proverbs called the Adagia, in which he recorded the Latin saying In regione caecorum rex est luscus: in the country of the blind, the one eyed man is king (Erasmus 335). Five centuries later, Steven Spielberg places that same proverb at the center of a film obsessed with eyeballs, surveillance, and the politics of who is allowed to see. Minority Report (2002), Spielberg's adaptation of a 1956 short story by Philip K. Dick, is set in a 2054 Washington, D.C. in which a Department of Precrime arrests citizens for murders they have not yet committed, on the strength of visions produced by three captive seers known as Precogs. The film is dominated by images of mechanical, institutional sight: optical scanners that read citizens' retinas as they walk through subway stations, holographic crime previews replayed in slow motion, and the Precogs themselves floating in a milk colored bath while their visions are extracted by machines. Yet the film is equally insistent on a second register of imagery, one that the Precrime apparatus cannot process at all: the small, embodied, deeply private images of grief that haunt the chief of Precrime, John Anderton, whose young son disappeared years before the film begins. In Minority Report, Spielberg uses this contrast between the cold, mechanical visual imagery of state surveillance and the fragile, embodied imagery of personal loss to argue that any system promising perfect sight is in fact blind to the human costs it cannot predict, prevent, or repair.

The first half of Minority Report is dominated by visual imagery that presents seeing itself as a state function, mechanical, institutional, and stripped of intimacy. The opening Precrime sequence establishes the register immediately: three pale figures float in a shallow pool inside a circular chamber the Precrime officers literally call "the temple," their eyes rolled back as their visions are pulled out of their skulls and projected as silvery, half formed images on a curved glass screen (Spielberg 00:04:18 to 00:06:42). The space is cold, blue lit, and almost ecclesiastical, but the ritual it stages is technological, not spiritual: the Precogs are not consulted, they are harvested. Anderton scrubs back and forth through their visions on a transparent display, treating future murders the way an editor treats raw footage. Sight, in this opening, is something the state extracts from human bodies and replays at will. Spielberg extends the same logic into public space a few minutes later, when a swarm of small mechanical "spider" drones is released into a tenement building and crawls under doors, climbing onto sleeping faces to scan each resident's retina in turn (Spielberg 01:09:30 to 01:11:00). The shot pulls back to a god's eye view of an entire apartment block with each unit lit up as the spiders pass through it, reducing every citizen to a single optical signature that the state has already filed, sorted, and made searchable. The image is unforgettable precisely because it makes seeing one directional: the state looks at the citizen, and the citizen, asleep, cannot look back. Geoffrey Bakewell argues that the film's persistent imagery of vision and blindness is not incidental but structural, pointing out that "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" because "in each, a powerful detective reopens an unsolved homicide case and is himself subsequently accused of murder" (95). Bakewell's reading clarifies the stakes of the spider scene and the temple sequence taken together: the visual imagery of mechanical sight is not a neutral aesthetic choice but the film's central argument turned into image, the claim that a society which sees too well has already begun to mistake its own gaze for justice.

Against this institutional register of sight, Spielberg builds a second image system that the Precrime apparatus is structurally incapable of processing: the small, private, grieving imagery of a father remembering a child the system could not save. The most devastating example arrives early. Alone in his apartment, Anderton sits in the dark and watches a holographic home recording of his missing son, Sean, projected into the room from a small disc; he reaches into the shimmering image as the boy laughs and runs, but his hand passes through the light and the projection scatters around him (Spielberg 00:21:40 to 00:23:55). The shot is the exact inverse of the Precog visions in the opening sequence. Where the temple footage is institutional, monochrome, and consumed by Anderton at his desk like surveillance video, the home recording is warm, private, and physically out of reach: an image of love that he can see but cannot touch. The film makes the contrast unmistakable. The state can replay a future murder in slow motion, but it cannot return the one child whose absence drives the protagonist's grief. Critic David Edelstein argues that the film ultimately trades the bleak political warning of Philip K. Dick's original story for a more sentimental humanism, less a protest against an Orwellian police state than a plea on behalf of the three enslaved Precogs, whose freedom matters more to the film than the freedom of all the citizens the system surveils. Edelstein's reading is sharp, but the home video sequence complicates it: the film's deepest sympathy belongs not only to the Precogs but to the people, like Anderton, whose private losses the surveillance state cannot perceive at all. That blindness becomes literal at the film's structural pivot, when the retired scientist Iris Hineman, working in a greenhouse full of carnivorous plants, looks up at Anderton and tells him: "In the land of the blind, the one eyed man is king" (Spielberg 01:14:22). Coming from the woman who helped invent the Precog program, the line is not a celebration of partial sight but an indictment of a country that has handed over its eyes to a machine. In his career retrospective on the film, Brian Eggert argues that what Spielberg does with the title concept is convert the eponymous "minority report," the suppressed alternative vision stored in the Precog Agatha's unconscious, from a throwaway plot device into a sustained philosophical challenge to the entire idea that the future can be known in advance, and that the film's persistent imagery of fragile, partial, embodied sight (a hand passing through a hologram, a single divergent vision buried in one woman's mind) is the means by which it makes that case. Eggert's reading clarifies why the home video and the greenhouse line carry so much weight in a film otherwise dominated by spectacle: the small, grieving images are the film's evidence that the apparatus, for all its slow motion clarity, has been looking in the wrong direction all along.

• Conclusion
① Creative Opening
Does not begin with "In conclusion." Opens with a verifiable production fact: Spielberg's 1999 futurist think tank.
② Film Referenced
Brief reference to the film's final image as a frame.
③ Thesis Restated
In new words; does not repeat the introduction's phrasing.
④ Broader Significance
Extends the argument from 2054 fiction to the surveillance present.
⑤ Memorable Closing Sentence
Returns to the Erasmus framing from the introduction.

In 1999, three years before Minority Report reached theaters, Steven Spielberg convened fifteen futurists, computer scientists, urbanists, and authors, including Peter Schwartz of the Global Business Network, virtual reality pioneer Jaron Lanier, and architect Peter Calthorpe, for a three day summit at a Santa Monica hotel and asked them to imagine, in plausible technological detail, the city of 2054 ("Minority Report"). What that summit produced was a city that could see almost everything: a city of retinal scanners, predictive policing, and personalized advertising that calls each passerby by name. The film ends not with that city in ruins but with the Precrime program quietly shut down and the three Precogs released to a small wooden cabin where, the camera lingers, they read books in the firelight (Spielberg 02:08:30). Nothing about the surveillance world is undone. What changes is the film's relationship to sight itself: it returns the act of seeing to small, private rooms, to the act of reading by lamplight, to the kind of gaze that does not require a database. Spielberg's two image systems, the cold, institutional vision of Precrime and the fragile, embodied vision of grief and ordinary attention, do not resolve into a single picture; they remain in tension, and that tension is the argument. Minority Report insists that a society which builds machines to see for it has not gained sight but lost it, because the things most worth seeing, a child running through a hologram, a face asleep behind a closed door, the buried minority report of a woman who knew better, were never going to register on any apparatus designed to predict crime. Five centuries after Erasmus copied down his proverb about the kingdom of the blind, Spielberg returns the line to us with the warning embedded in the original: in a country that has given its eyes away, the one who can still see at all is the one who refuses to look away from what cannot be measured.

✎ Works Cited Format Notes

Alphabetical order: Entries are arranged by the author's last name. Films with no individual author at the front of the entry are alphabetized by title, ignoring "A," "An," and "The."

Hanging indent: The second and subsequent lines of each entry are indented half an inch, the opposite of a paragraph indent.

Citing a film: For a film, MLA 9 lists the title in italics, followed by the director, key performers, the production companies, and the year. In text citations use timestamps in the format (Spielberg 01:14:22).

Database entries: When a source was accessed through an academic database, add the database name (italicized) and the URL or stable link as a second container at the end of the entry.

PDF vs. HTML sources: If a source is a PDF or print document with page numbers, include the page number in the parenthetical citation: (Bakewell 95). If a source is an HTML webpage with no page numbers, name the author in the signal phrase and use no parenthetical at all.

Every cited source appears: Your primary source (the film) plus every secondary source you cite in the essay, including any brief historical or production facts, must appear on the Works Cited.

SEC · 03

Revising Your Draft

Revising is not proofreading. Proofreading (fixing spelling, grammar, and formatting) comes in Stage Four. Revision is rethinking and reshaping: reading your draft with fresh eyes to ask whether the argument holds, whether the evidence supports it, and whether every paragraph does what you said it would do. The four revision questions below give you a framework for that rereading.

1
Argument and Thesis
  • Does your thesis make a specific, arguable claim about the film, not just a summary of what it is about?
  • Does every body paragraph advance that claim, or does any paragraph drift into plot description without analysis?
  • Does your conclusion restate the thesis in new words and add a broader reflection?
2
Primary Source Evidence
  • Does each body paragraph contain 2 to 3 brief, specific references to the film with timestamps?
  • Are all film references introduced with a signal phrase or context before the citation?
  • Is each cited shot or line followed by your own analysis, not just a restatement of what happens?
  • Are timestamps cited correctly in the format (Director Last Name 00:00:00)?
3
Secondary Source Integration
  • Have you used all three required types: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary?
  • Is the PDF / database source cited with a page number in parentheses?
  • Are both HTML sources cited correctly: author named in signal phrase, no parenthetical at all?
  • After each secondary source, does your analysis explain how it connects to your argument?
4
Structure and Flow
  • Does your introduction open creatively (with a documented quote, fact, or framing) and end with the thesis?
  • Does each body paragraph open with a topic sentence that names the paragraph's specific focus?
  • Does each body paragraph close with a sentence that ties its evidence back to the thesis?
  • Does your conclusion avoid introducing new quotes or new evidence beyond a brief returning reference?
