Orientation

Before the Play: A World of Stone and Iron

When Shakespeare wrote Hamlet around 1600, he set it in a Denmark that bore no resemblance to the England of Elizabeth I — and he knew it. The world of the play reaches back six centuries before Shakespeare's own time, to a Scandinavian culture of longhouses and longships, of blood feuds and dynastic murder, of a Christianity still new enough to sit uneasily beside older ways of reckoning with death and revenge.

Understanding Hamlet's world — not Shakespeare's London, but Amleth's Denmark — changes how you read the play. The ghost does not walk the battlements of a Renaissance palace. He walks the ramparts of a Norse stronghold on a headland above the Øresund strait, in a world where the demand for revenge was not merely emotional but legal, social, and sacred.

A Note on Names

The historical figure behind Shakespeare's Hamlet is Amleth — a prince of the Danes whose story was recorded by the Danish chronicler Saxo Grammaticus in his Gesta Danorum (c. 1200 CE). Shakespeare transformed Amleth into Hamlet, Denmark into a Renaissance court, and a saga of brutal political survival into one of the most psychologically complex works in Western literature. This page is about Amleth's world. The differences between that world and Shakespeare's are where the most interesting questions live.

Geography

The World of the Danes

The Denmark of Hamlet's world was not the tidy modern nation-state of today. It was the dominant power of the North Sea world — a seafaring kingdom whose territory included the Jutland peninsula, the Danish islands, the southern tip of the Scandinavian peninsula (modern southern Sweden), and sphere of influence extending from England to the Baltic.

The castle of Elsinore — in Danish, Helsingør — sits at the narrowest point of the Øresund strait, the passage between the North Sea and the Baltic. This location was not incidental. Whoever held Helsingør controlled the movement of every ship between the two seas, and could levy tolls on all of northern European trade. The castle was a strategic instrument of enormous economic and political power.

Shakespeare inherited this geography from his sources and preserved it. But the Elsinore of the historical Amleth was not the Renaissance castle Spielberg would imagine — it was a fortified headland, timber and stone, exposed to North Sea weather, at the edge of a world that was only beginning to call itself Christian.

The Øresund Strait

At its narrowest point, the strait between Denmark and Sweden is just 4 kilometers wide. On clear days, Helsingør and Helsingborg — on opposite shores — are plainly visible to one another. The ghost's world is a world of maritime watchfulness: a fortress overlooking a passage that the whole of northern trade depends on.

Historical Context

The Norse World of Amleth

Viking Age Denmark Suggested: Jelling runestones / Trelleborg ring fortress / Norse longhouse reconstruction
Viking Age Denmark · 10th century

The historical Amleth belongs to the Viking Age — a period roughly spanning 793 to 1066 CE — when Scandinavian culture was defined by maritime expansion, dynastic violence, a complex polytheistic religion, and a social code in which honor, revenge, and kinship obligation were not personal preferences but binding law.

This is a world governed by the thing — the public assembly where disputes were settled, laws declared, and kings confirmed. It is a world in which murder within a family created a crisis not just for the family but for the entire political order, because the killing of a king ruptured the covenant between the ruler, his people, and the forces — divine or otherwise — that guaranteed the harvest, the weather, and the outcome of battles.

The demand for revenge in this world was not the hot, personal emotion we might imagine. It was a legal and cosmic obligation. To fail to avenge a murdered father was not weakness — it was a breach of the fundamental order of things. This is the world in which Amleth's hesitation would have been not philosophically interesting but socially catastrophic.

I

Kinship & Blood Obligation

In Norse society, the family group — the ætt — was the primary unit of social and legal existence. Crimes against one member were crimes against the whole. The obligation to avenge a murdered kinsman was not optional; it was the defining test of a family's honor and its continued standing in the world.

II

The Hall & Feasting Culture

Political life centered on the mead-hall — the great timber longhouse where lord and retainers ate, drank, made oaths, heard poets, and demonstrated loyalty. The hall was the physical site of power. Control of the hall meant control of the kingdom. Claudius's court, with its elaborate feasts and toasts, inherits this tradition directly.

III

The Transition to Christianity

By the 10th–11th century, Denmark was in the process of Christianization — King Harald Bluetooth's famous runestone at Jelling (c. 965 CE) records that he "made the Danes Christian." But conversion was uneven, contested, and incomplete. Old Norse beliefs — about the dead, about fate, about the obligations of the living to the slain — persisted beneath the new theology. Hamlet's ghost exists in exactly this fault line.

Primary Source

Saxo Grammaticus & the Gesta Danorum

Gesta Danorum Saxo Grammaticus · c. 1200 · Manuscript page Suggested: medieval manuscript page / Saxo's original Latin text
Gesta Danorum · c. 1200 CE

The History of the Danes

Around 1200 CE — roughly 300 years after the events it describes, and 400 years before Shakespeare — a Danish cleric named Saxo Grammaticus compiled the Gesta Danorum (History of the Danes), a vast Latin chronicle of Danish kings and legendary heroes. Books III and IV contain the story of Amleth, prince of Jutland.

Saxo's Amleth is a figure of ruthless political calculation, not philosophical paralysis. He feigns madness to survive — not as a symptom of existential crisis, but as a deliberate, cold strategy to stay alive long enough to take revenge. His uncle Feng has murdered his father and married his mother. Amleth bides his time. When he acts, he acts decisively and with considerable violence.

Shakespeare read Saxo's story (most likely through a 1570 French adaptation by François de Belleforest) and transformed it into something Saxo would barely recognize. The plot of survival and revenge remains. Everything else — the interiority, the doubt, the philosophy — Shakespeare invented.

"Amleth, when he was come to man's estate, showed plainly that the veil of dullness had covered a most penetrating mind."
Saxo Grammaticus · Gesta Danorum · c. 1200 CE · trans. Oliver Elton

Historical Timeline

From Amleth to Hamlet

The story we call Hamlet passed through five centuries and at least three major retellings before it reached Shakespeare's stage. Each transformation reflected the world in which it was told.

c. 900–1000
The Historical Amleth
Historical

The probable historical events underlying the legend: a dynastic struggle among Norse lords in Jutland. The broad outlines — fratricide, usurped throne, feigned madness, delayed revenge — suggest a real political crisis, though no contemporary records survive. The story entered oral tradition as a saga of survival and blood justice.

c. 965
Harald Bluetooth Christianizes Denmark
Historical

The Jelling runestone — erected by King Harald Bluetooth — records the formal Christianization of Denmark. The old Norse religion begins its long retreat. The theological framework that will make the ghost's demand for revenge morally complicated does not yet exist — but it is beginning to be built.

c. 1185–1208
Saxo Grammaticus — Gesta Danorum
Primary Source

A Danish cleric compiles the History of the Danes in Latin. Books III–IV contain the Amleth legend in its first written form. Saxo's Amleth is strategic, ruthless, and ultimately successful. He burns his enemies alive in the feasting hall, kills his uncle, and becomes king. Shakespeare does not give Hamlet this ending.

1570
Belleforest — Histoires Tragiques
Source

French writer François de Belleforest adapts Saxo's Amleth story into a moral tale in his collection of tragic histories. Belleforest amplifies Amleth's interiority slightly and adds a stronger moral framework — the story is now about virtue tested, not merely survival. This is almost certainly the version Shakespeare knew.

c. 1589
The Ur-Hamlet
Pre-Shakespeare

References in contemporary documents suggest an earlier English play about Hamlet — possibly by Thomas Kyd — existed before Shakespeare's version. It is now lost. Shakespeare may have been revising an existing theatrical property, not working entirely from scratch.

c. 1600–1601
Shakespeare — The Tragedy of Hamlet, Prince of Denmark
Shakespeare

Shakespeare sets his version in a court that looks and sounds Elizabethan despite its Danish name — and transforms the saga's ruthless survivor into a figure of radical psychological complexity. The Norse world of blood obligation and dynastic violence is still present in the plot's skeleton. But Shakespeare drapes it in Renaissance philosophy, Protestant theology, and a language of interiority that had no equivalent in Saxo's world.

Then & Now

Amleth & Hamlet: Two Worlds

The gap between Saxo's Amleth and Shakespeare's Hamlet is not just a gap between characters — it is a gap between entire worldviews. The comparison below maps the most significant differences.

Why the Ghost Changes Everything

Saxo's Amleth does not need a ghost because he already knows his father was murdered. The information is not hidden. The question is not "what happened?" but "when and how do I act?"

Shakespeare's ghost introduces a theological crisis that Saxo's world could not have imagined. In Protestant England (where purgatory had been abolished), a ghost claiming to be the spirit of a dead man was automatically suspect — it might be a devil in disguise, luring Hamlet toward damnation. Hamlet's hesitation is not weakness. It is, among other things, good theology.

What Denmark Still Means

Shakespeare chose to keep the Danish setting even as he transformed everything else about the story. Denmark remained — for Elizabethan audiences — a place of darkness, cold, and moral ambiguity. It was recent enough in memory as a Viking world that the setting carried associations of violence, political ruthlessness, and a different relationship to death.

The world you are about to read is layered: Saxo's Norse saga underneath, Shakespeare's Renaissance court on the surface, and the gap between them where the play's most interesting questions live.
