Step 2 Analyze the Poem's Imagery

Use the four-step imagery analysis method from the Defining Poetic Imagery page. Here are the steps as a quick reference:

Step 1: Identify

Identify the poem's key images: which words or phrases create vivid sensory pictures?

Step 2: Engage

Engage the images by writing down their connotative meanings and associations.

Step 3: Connect

Look for patterns among the images and link them to any key statement in the poem.

Step 4: Formulate

Formulate a basic interpretation of the poem grounded in your image analysis.

Reminder

Use the sample analyses of “The Red Wheelbarrow” and “Morning Song” on the previous page as your models for how to complete each step.

Below is a complete sample response for Sylvia Plath's “Morning Song” to use as your model.

Sample Response — Imagery Analysis
Sylvia Plath's “Morning Song”
Key Images

“fat gold watch,” “statue,” “drafty museum,” “cloud . . . distills a mirror,” “mouth open clean as a cat's,” “handful of notes,” “clear vowels rise like balloons.”

Connotative Meanings

Watch suggests mechanical life; time; baby joins the world of nature/elements. Effacement: “thinning of the cervix during labor.” Speaker feels like she's disappearing after giving birth? Images suggest emotional distance? Baby's like a statue; not real; something to be stared at from a distance. Baby's crying now like music; balloons are light, airy.

Connections & Patterns Linked to Ideas

The conflicting images suggest the mother is emotionally distant to her child but still sees something positive in him or her.

Basic Interpretation

In “Morning Song,” Sylvia Plath depicts two aspects of a new mother's experience: a surprising emotional disconnection from her baby and a sense of possible hope at the future. Some of the visual images associated with her newborn suggest a lack of feelings towards her child: she pictures her infant as being like a watch, a statue, a moth, and a cat, none of which are actually human. This could perhaps hint at the confusion or fear she's feeling at being a new mother. However, she explicitly claims in one line to not be the baby's mother at all, which implies a more serious emotional distance. This contrasts with the more common idea many have of all mothers being instantly attached emotionally to their children and fully prepared to raise them. Some other images, however, hint that maybe the mother remains optimistic. These images are related to the sense of sound. The poem's title refers to her baby's crying as a “song,” which is a positive image. Also, at the end, when her child begins crying, she says the sound is like balloons floating into the air, another positive image suggesting lightness and possible future birthdays. This subtle sense of optimism leaves one with the impression that the mother is hopeful she'll eventually be able to bond more strongly with her baby.

Evaluation & Submission Details

Grading Rubric

The Discussion Post Grading Rubric will be used to assess this assignment. Your analysis will be evaluated on how thoroughly each of the four steps is completed. Use the two sample analyses — “The Red Wheelbarrow” and “Morning Song” — as models. Remember that interpreting imagery is subjective: there is no single right or wrong interpretation. Be sure to edit and proofread for sentence, grammar, mechanical, and punctuation errors.
