💬 Introducing Your Secondary Sources — Signal Phrase Tips

Secondary source quotes carry more weight when you introduce them with signal phrases that name the scholar and establish their authority — rather than generic openers like “According to an article” or, worse, no introduction at all. Here are the two approaches:

✅ Stronger: Name the Scholar

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112).

Naming the scholar and describing their role signals to readers that this is an expert perspective. Notice: author named in signal phrase → page number only in the citation.

⚠️ Weaker: Anonymous Introduction

According to an article, Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd 112).

This doesn’t tell readers who is making the argument or why they’re worth listening to. Name the scholar in the signal phrase — don’t bury them in the parenthetical.

Making a claim:
argues • asserts
claims • contends
maintains • insists
Explaining or describing:
explains • notes
observes • describes
points out • states
Analyzing or interpreting:
analyzes • interprets
examines • reads
suggests • reveals
Emphasizing:
emphasizes • highlights
stresses • underscores
demonstrates • shows

💡 Use present tense with these verbs: “Boyd argues” — not “Boyd argued.” Published scholarly work is treated as always present.

Section 3
✏️ MLA Documentation: Paraphrasing Your Secondary Sources

And now you get to learn about one of the most frequent reasons students get accused of plagiarism — the act of incorporating the ideas or language of another person without properly acknowledging it.

Paraphrasing is a popular and effective technique for incorporating ideas from a source without relying on lengthy direct quotes. Unlike direct quotes, a paraphrase is not word-for-word. Instead, you’re incorporating the author’s idea — but completely rewriting it in your own words. Paraphrases are trickier to execute than direct quotes, though, and the consequences of doing them wrong can occasionally be serious.

The most common error is what the plagiarism page called Find and Replace Plagiarism: swapping individual words for synonyms while keeping the original sentence structure intact. This is not a paraphrase. The handout below explains the difference and walks through complete examples.
