💡 You're Looking for the Literature Subject

Since you're writing a literary analysis essay, all of your secondary sources will come from the Literature subject databases — not Arts & Humanities or any other subject category. The key databases to look for include Literary Reference Center Plus, Literature Resource Center, and JSTOR.

💡 Step Three: Search Smarter — Tips Before You Start

Before you start clicking through databases, spend two minutes thinking about how you'll search. Academic databases reward specific, well-constructed searches. The strategies below will help you find usable results faster and avoid the frustration of hundreds of irrelevant results — or none at all.

🔑
Use Specific Search Terms

Always search using both the author's last name and the title of the story. This narrows the results to scholarship about your specific work.

Example searches: "Hurston" + "Sweat"
"Carter" + "Company of Wolves"
"Roth" + "Conversion of the Jews"
🚫
Avoid These Mistakes

Broad searches return too many results; overly narrow searches return none.

  • Searching only a genre term like "short story analysis"
  • Searching the author's name alone without the title
  • Searching complete sentences or questions
  • Using Google instead of the library databases
✅
What a Good Source Looks Like

Once you find a result, quickly scan it before reading the whole thing:

  • It analyzes or interprets your specific story — not just summarizes it
  • It's written by a scholar or critic, not a student
  • It appears in an academic journal or reputable publication
  • The abstract mentions your story and makes an argument about it
📚
Don't Forget eBooks

The library also provides access to eBooks — books available electronically on your computer. These can be used as secondary sources for literary criticism of your chosen story.

Access eBooks using the eBook links in the Literature Database list, or search the library's homepage directly.
⚡ Quick Check: Is This Source Acceptable for My Essay?
✅ Yes — It interprets or analyzes the specific short story I'm writing about
✅ Yes — It's published in an academic journal or by a university press
❌ No — It's mainly a biography or life history of the author, not an analysis of the story
❌ No — It's a general internet article, a plot summary, or a Wikipedia-style entry
❌ No — It's about a different work by the same author, not the story I'm analyzing
