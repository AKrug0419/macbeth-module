MLA Documentation • Secondary Sources

Quoting from Your Secondary Research Sources

The four citation patterns for scholarly and critical sources, plus the most common errors students make when weaving secondary voices into their film analysis.

Your three secondary sources may be PDF articles retrieved from an academic database (with page numbers) or HTML articles published on the web (no page numbers). The citation format changes depending on the source type and on where you place the author's name. There are four patterns in total. Once you learn all four, you can integrate any secondary source cleanly into your prose.

Part One
Core Skills
Rule 1

Signal Phrases for Secondary Sources

A signal phrase introduces a quotation by naming the author and providing context. Strong signal phrases establish the scholar's identity and authority. It is generally preferable to name the scholar in the signal phrase rather than hide the name inside the parenthetical: this gives the scholar credit and makes the sentence read more naturally. Alternate between placing the name in the signal phrase and placing it in the parenthetical so your paragraphs do not become repetitive.

Signal verbs to vary throughout your essay argues • claims • contends • maintains • suggests • observes • notes • explains • writes • points out • concludes • asserts • demonstrates • shows

Always use present tense for signal verbs with literary and film criticism: "Bakewell argues," not "Bakewell argued." A scholar's argument stays active in the present tense, even if the article was published decades ago.

✓ Author introduced by name and role

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that Spielberg's film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" because "in each, a powerful detective reopens an unsolved homicide case and is himself subsequently accused of murder" (95).

Naming the scholar and identifying the scholar's field establishes authority and makes the source feel purposeful.
✓ Author name in parenthetical instead

Scholars of Minority Report have noted that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus," since "a powerful detective reopens an unsolved homicide case and is himself subsequently accused of murder" (Bakewell 95).

Name omitted from the signal phrase, so it must appear in the parenthetical citation before the page number.
Rule 2

Pattern A • PDF Article, Author's Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is a PDF article (one you downloaded from a database such as Project MUSE, JSTOR, or EBSCOhost) and you have not named the author in your signal phrase, include both the author's last name and the page number inside the parenthetical citation. No comma separates them. The period falls after the closing parenthesis.

Format "quotation" (LastName PageNumber).  →  e.g., (Bakewell 95)
✗ Comma between name and page number

Scholars of Minority Report have argued that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell, 95).

Never place a comma between the author's last name and the page number in MLA format.
✓ Correct, no comma

Scholars of Minority Report have argued that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell 95).

✓ Two-author source

Critics have observed that the spider drone sequence "stages the surveillance state not as distant abstraction but as something that crawls onto your sleeping face" (Schatz and Perkins 142).

Two-author source: use both last names connected with "and." This format applies if you happen to find a co-authored film studies article.
Rule 3

Pattern B • PDF Article, Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is a PDF article and you have named the author in the signal phrase, the parenthetical citation contains the page number only. The author's name is not repeated.

Format Author argues that "quotation" (PageNumber).  →  e.g., Bakewell argues that "…" (95).
✗ Name repeated in citation

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that Spielberg's film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell 95).

Author already named in the signal phrase, so do not repeat the name in the citation. Page number only.
✓ Correct, page number only

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that Spielberg's film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

Rule 4

Pattern C • HTML Article, Author's Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is an HTML article (one published on a website such as Slate, Deep Focus Review, or The New Yorker) and you have not named the author in your signal phrase, include only the author's last name in the parenthetical citation. There is no page number. Do not invent or estimate one.

Format "quotation" (LastName).  →  e.g., (Edelstein) or (Eggert)
✗ Invented page number for HTML source

Critics have argued that the film "trades the bleak political warning of Philip K. Dick's original story for a more sentimental humanism" (Edelstein 4).

HTML articles have no fixed pages. Do not estimate or invent a page number.
✓ Correct, author only, no page number

Critics have argued that the film "trades the bleak political warning of Philip K. Dick's original story for a more sentimental humanism" (Edelstein).

Rule 5

Pattern D • HTML Article, Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is an HTML article and you have named the author in the signal phrase, no parenthetical citation is needed at all. Simply end the sentence with a period after the closing quotation mark.

Format Author argues that "quotation."  →  No parenthetical needed.
Important Note on Punctuation

When there is no parenthetical citation, American English convention places the period inside the closing quotation mark: "quotation." This is the one situation where a period appears inside the quotation marks. When a parenthetical citation follows, the period always goes after the closing parenthesis: "quotation" (Author Page).

✗ Unnecessary citation added

Critic David Edelstein argues that the film "trades the bleak political warning of Philip K. Dick's original story for a more sentimental humanism" (Edelstein).

Author already named in the signal phrase, so the parenthetical citation is redundant. Remove it.
✓ Correct, no parenthetical needed

Critic David Edelstein argues that the film "trades the bleak political warning of Philip K. Dick's original story for a more sentimental humanism."

✓ Another HTML plus signal phrase example

Film critic Brian Eggert notes that the suppressed alternative vision "stored in the Precog Agatha's unconscious" becomes "a sustained philosophical challenge to the entire idea that the future can be known in advance."

HTML source plus author in signal phrase equals no parenthetical citation needed.
Rule 6

Articles With No Identified Author

Occasionally, an article will not list a named author. The most common example for film research is a Wikipedia entry or an unsigned studio press release. In that case, use the first one to three significant words of the article's title inside the parenthetical citation, in quotation marks. These sources are relatively rare in ENC 1102, and it is generally preferable to choose sources with identifiable authors, since named scholars and critics carry more weight.

Format "quotation" ("First Words").  ← HTML  |  "quotation" ("First Words" PageNumber).  ← PDF
✓ HTML source, no author

Production accounts of Minority Report record that Spielberg "convened a three day summit in 1999 at a Santa Monica hotel" and asked the participants "to imagine the technologies of 2054" ("Minority Report").

No author identified, so use the first significant words of the title in quotation marks. HTML source, so no page number.
SEC 02

Quick Reference • The Four Citation Patterns

Every secondary source citation in your essay will follow one of these four patterns. Keep this reference open while you draft.

Pattern A
PDF, author name in citation
"…" (Bakewell 95).
Pattern B
PDF, author name in signal phrase
Bakewell argues "…" (95).
Pattern C
HTML, author name in citation
"…" (Edelstein).
Pattern D
HTML, author name in signal phrase
Edelstein argues "…."
The single most common error

Repeating the author's name in both the signal phrase and the citation. Pick one location for the name and use it there only. See Rule 11 below.

Part Two
Common Quoting Errors
Rule 7 Common Error

Isolated or Floating Quotes

A floating quote is a quotation that stands alone as its own sentence without being introduced or integrated into your own prose. Every secondary source quotation must be attached to your own words before and after: a signal phrase establishes who is speaking and why the words matter, and your own analysis explains what the evidence contributes to your argument.

✗ Floating quote

The film is obsessed with eyes. "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell 95). Spielberg uses this to show how surveillance works.

The quote stands alone with no signal phrase and only a vague sentence following it.
✓ Quote introduced and followed by analysis

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95). Bakewell's reading clarifies the stakes of the spider drone sequence: the visual imagery of mechanical sight is not a neutral aesthetic choice but the film's central argument turned into image, the claim that a society which sees too well has already begun to mistake its own gaze for justice.

The Signal → Quote → Analysis pattern

Every secondary source use follows the same three-part structure: (1) a signal phrase that names the scholar and establishes context, (2) the quoted or paraphrased material, and (3) your own sentence or two of analysis connecting the scholar's point to your argument. The analysis is not optional.

Rule 8 Common Error

Comma Splices Involving Quotes

A common quoting error is using a comma to join your own complete independent clause to a quotation. If your lead-in is a complete sentence, use a colon, not a comma. Alternatively, restructure the lead-in so the quotation flows naturally from an incomplete construction, which is usually more elegant.

✗ Comma between complete clause and quote

Bakewell makes an important point about this scene, "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

"Bakewell makes an important point" is a complete sentence. A comma cannot join it to the quotation.
✓ Colon after complete lead-in

Bakewell makes an important point about this scene: "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

✓ Restructured so quote flows from incomplete lead-in

Bakewell connects the film to classical tragedy, arguing that "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

"Bakewell connects the film to classical tragedy, arguing that" is incomplete, so the quote flows naturally. No colon needed.
Rule 9 Common Error

Colon Before a Quote • Signal Phrase Must Be a Complete Sentence

A colon may introduce a quotation only when the signal phrase is a complete sentence. If your lead-in is an incomplete construction (such as "Bakewell notes that" or "According to Edelstein"), do not use a colon. Let the quotation flow directly.

✗ Colon after incomplete lead-in

Bakewell notes that: "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

"Bakewell notes that" is not a complete sentence. A colon cannot follow it.
✓ No colon, quote flows naturally

Bakewell notes that "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

✓ Colon correct after a complete sentence

Bakewell identifies the film's central structural conceit: "Minority Report is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

"Bakewell identifies the film's central structural conceit" is a complete sentence, so the colon is correct.
Rule 10 Common Error

Punctuation Left Inside the Quotation Marks

When a quotation is followed by a parenthetical citation, do not leave the original text's ending punctuation inside the closing quotation mark. The exceptions are question marks and exclamation marks that belong to the quoted material itself. These stay inside.

✗ Original period left inside

Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus." (95).

The period from the original text should not appear here, and this also creates double punctuation.
✓ Original period removed

Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

✓ Exception, question mark stays inside

Eggert poses a central question about the film's closing image: "Does Anderton's reunion with his wife actually earn its optimism, or does the surveillance state simply recede into the background?"

Question marks from the original text stay inside the quotation marks. Since this is an HTML source with the author named in the signal phrase, no parenthetical follows.
Rule 11 Common Error

Author's Name Appearing in Both Signal Phrase and Citation

The author's name belongs in one place only: either in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation, never in both. When the name appears in the signal phrase, the citation contains only the page number (PDF) or nothing (HTML). Repeating the name in both places is redundant and incorrect.

✗ Name repeated in signal phrase and citation

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell 95).

Author named in the signal phrase, so remove the name from the citation. Page number only: (95).
✓ Correct, name in signal phrase only

Classicist Geoffrey Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

✓ Correct, name in citation only

One scholar connects the film directly to classical tragedy, arguing that it "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (Bakewell 95).

No name in the signal phrase, so the last name must appear in the citation.
Rule 12 Common Error

Missing End Punctuation After a Quote

When a quotation ends your sentence, the sentence still needs its own closing punctuation (a period, semicolon, or colon) placed after the parenthetical citation. When there is no parenthetical citation (HTML source, author in signal phrase), the period goes inside the closing quotation mark instead.

✗ Missing period after citation

Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95)

The sentence ends after the closing parenthesis with no period.
✓ Correct, period after citation

Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95).

✓ Semicolon after citation, joining two clauses

Bakewell argues that the film "is a legitimate child of Oedipus Tyrannus" (95); this reading clarifies why the Precrime apparatus is doomed from its opening shot to become the very thing it claims to prevent.

A semicolon after the citation is correct when joining two independent clauses.
