Assignment Instructions

Two Parts, Three Sources

For your Poetry & Literary Elements Research Essay, you'll be working with three secondary sources, the same number you used for your previous research essay. This time, however, you'll be locating them using two different methods: one through a literary criticism database and two through internet research, using the search strategies and evaluation tools you studied in this module.

This assignment has two parts. In Part One, you'll submit MLA-formatted citations for all three sources. In Part Two, you'll write a brief SIFT-based evaluation for each of your two internet sources, demonstrating how you verified them before deciding to use them.

A Note on Source Types

All three sources should be literary criticism, secondary sources that analyze, interpret, or discuss the poem (or the poet's work) you're writing about. The same requirements that applied to your previous research essay apply here: your sources should be written by scholars or critics with relevant expertise, published in academic or established literary contexts, and directly relevant to your essay's argument.

Part Two
SIFT Evaluation of Your Internet Sources

For each of your two internet sources, write a brief evaluation using the four prompts below. Work through each prompt in order, labeling your response for each one. Your evaluation of each source should be at least 150 words.

You are demonstrating that you actually investigated these sources before deciding to use them, not just that they look credible at first glance. Your evaluations should be specific: name the websites, publications, or search results you found during your lateral investigation, not just general impressions.

Answer Each of the Following for Both Internet Sources
  • S
    Stop: How You Found It
    What search engine and search terms did you use to find this source? What was your initial impression of it, and what made you decide it was worth investigating further?
    If you used an AI tool to generate search keywords, briefly describe how you used it and what terms it suggested.
  • I
    Investigate: Your Lateral Research
    What did you find when you left the page and searched for the source, publication, or author in a new browser tab? Who publishes this source, and what did other sources tell you about its credibility?
    Be specific: name what you searched for and what you found. "It looked legitimate" is not a sufficient answer here.
  • F
    Find: Corroboration
    What other credible sources corroborate this source's focus or arguments? Does the scholarly conversation about your poem support the general direction this source takes?
    You don't need sources that make exactly the same argument, just evidence that the critical conversation this source participates in is real and established.
  • T
    Trace: Credibility Conclusion
    Based on your investigation, what specific evidence makes you confident this source is credible and appropriate for an academic research essay? What would have disqualified it?
    Consider: author credentials, publisher reputation, citation practices within the source, and whether any claims seemed unverifiable.

PART TWO: SIFT EVALUATIONS Complete a SIFT evaluation for each internet source

For each source, all four SIFT letters are addressed in order. Responses name specific search terms, websites, and publications, not general impressions.

Internet Source One (Source 2) : SIFT Evaluation
S
Stop What search engine and search terms did you use? What was your first impression?
If you used an AI tool to suggest keywords, briefly describe how and what terms it offered.
I searched Google using the terms Sylvia Plath “Morning Song” confessional poetry critical analysis. The first page was dominated by student essay sites and SparkNotes-style summaries, so I scrolled past those. The Modern American Poetry site appeared on the second page of results. The URL showed a .org domain with a reference to the University of Illinois, which suggested institutional sponsorship. The page listed essays by named scholars, not anonymous summaries, and included an essay by Diane Wood Middlebrook, whose name I recognized in connection with confessional poetry scholarship. That combination made me decide it was worth a closer look.
I
Investigate What did you find when you left the page and searched the source or author in a new tab?
Name the specific websites, publications, or results you found. “It looked legitimate” is not sufficient.
I opened a new tab and searched Modern American Poetry University of Illinois English department. The site is maintained by the Department of English at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign and has served as a standard scholarly resource for decades. Multiple university course syllabi linked to it, and entries appeared in the works cited sections of peer-reviewed articles I found through JSTOR. I then searched for Diane Wood Middlebrook and found that she was a professor of English at Stanford University and the author of Anne Sexton: A Biography (1991), widely cited in confessional poetry scholarship. Her faculty page was no longer live, she passed away in 2007, but her biography and publication record were confirmed through Stanford’s archived English department page and WorldCat.
F
Find What other credible sources corroborate this source’s focus or arguments?
You don’t need exact agreement, just evidence the critical conversation it joins is real and established.
My search in Bloom’s Literature returned several essays discussing confessional poetics and Plath’s relationship to that tradition, including entries that cited Middlebrook’s biography and critical writing directly. The term “confessional poetry,” central to Middlebrook’s argument, recurs consistently across the scholarship I found, in JSTOR articles on Plath, in the Axelrod essay from Bloom’s, and in multiple encyclopedia entries on postwar American poetry. The critical conversation Middlebrook is contributing to is clearly established and ongoing.
T
Trace What specific evidence makes you confident this source is credible for academic use?
Consider: author credentials, publisher reputation, citation practices, and any unverifiable claims.
I am confident this source is appropriate for academic use for three reasons: the credibility of the host institution (University of Illinois), the author’s verified scholarly standing (Stanford professor, widely cited biography), and the fact that the essay originally appeared in a scholarly anthology published by Columbia University Press. What would have disqualified it: an anonymous author, a site with no institutional affiliation, or a page that simply summarized Plath’s life without advancing a critical argument. None of those problems were present. I also read beyond the first paragraph to confirm the essay engages in interpretive argument rather than just description.
Internet Source Two (Source 3) : SIFT Evaluation
S
Stop What search engine and search terms did you use? What was your first impression?
If you used an AI tool to suggest keywords, briefly describe how and what terms it offered.
I used Google Scholar with the search terms Plath “Morning Song” Ariel revision manuscript. Among approximately fifty results, this article appeared on the first page with a title that matched my essay’s focus closely, Plath’s process of shaping the Ariel collection, which includes “Morning Song.” The journal name, Plath Profiles, suggested a specialized scholarly focus, which was both a reason to investigate it and a reason to verify it carefully. The abstract previewed an argument about revision and editorial choices relevant to understanding the poem’s final form.
I
Investigate What did you find when you left the page and searched the source or author in a new tab?
Name the specific websites, publications, or results you found. “It looked legitimate” is not sufficient.
Before reading the article, I opened a new tab and searched Plath Profiles journal peer-reviewed academic. I found it is a peer-reviewed, open-access journal dedicated to Sylvia Plath scholarship, archived through DePauw University, with an editorial board that includes scholars from Oxford and the University of Exeter. I then searched for Tracy Brain and found that she is a professor of creative writing and English at Bath Spa University, with a published book, The Other Sylvia Plath (Longman, 2001), that appears in academic databases and is cited in other Plath scholarship. Her faculty page at Bath Spa confirmed her credentials and a sustained research focus on Plath.
F
Find What other credible sources corroborate this source’s focus or arguments?
You don’t need exact agreement, just evidence the critical conversation it joins is real and established.
My search in Bloom’s Literature and JSTOR for criticism on the Ariel manuscript returned multiple articles on Plath’s editorial process, several of which cited Brain’s book directly. The question of how Plath ordered and shaped the Ariel poems is a documented area of scholarly discussion, with contributions from Ted Hughes’s original edition, Karen Kukil’s archival scholarship, and multiple peer-reviewed articles on the subject. Brain’s article fits directly into that conversation rather than standing in isolation.
T
Trace What specific evidence makes you confident this source is credible for academic use?
Consider: author credentials, publisher reputation, citation practices, and any unverifiable claims.
The combination of a peer-reviewed journal archived through a university, a verifiable academic author with a published book on the same subject, and a clearly argued thesis grounded in primary manuscript sources makes me confident this source is appropriate for academic use. What would have disqualified it: an unverifiable author, a self-published blog without editorial oversight, or an article that simply described the poems without advancing a critical argument. None of those problems are present. I also read the first five pages to confirm the article directly addresses aspects of “Morning Song” and the Ariel manuscript relevant to my essay’s argument.
