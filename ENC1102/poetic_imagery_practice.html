Poem One
Ada Limón — "Instructions on Not Giving Up"

Ada Limón (b. 1976) is the 24th Poet Laureate of the United States. Known for her attention to the natural world and the resilience of ordinary life, her poetry is celebrated for its clarity and emotional directness.

1
Step 1 — Identify the Key Images
Read the poem and notice the language that paints pictures or activates the senses
Your task

Read "Instructions on Not Giving Up" below. As you read, ask yourself: which words or phrases create vivid sensory pictures? Which ones appeal to sight, sound, touch, or taste? Think through your answers, then click "Reveal Images" to see the model.

Instructions on Not Giving Up — Ada Limón
More than the fuchsia funnels breaking out
of the crabapple tree, more than the neighbor's
almost obscene display of cherry limbs shoving
their cotton candy-colored blossoms to the slate
sky of Spring rains, it's the greening of the trees
that really gets to me. When all the shock of white
and taffy, the world's baubles and trinkets, leave
the pavement strewn with the confetti of aftermath,
the leaves come. Patient, plodding, a green skin
growing over whatever winter did to us, a return
to the strange idea of continuous living despite
the mess of us, the hurt, the empty. Fine then,
I'll take it, the tree seems to say, a new slick leaf
unfurling like a fist to an open palm, I'll take it all.

Source: Poets.org

2
Step 2 — Connotations
What does each image make you think of? What feelings or ideas does it suggest?
🔒
Your task

For each key image below, ask: What does this image make me think of? What feelings, memories, or ideas does it bring up? Jot down your thoughts, then reveal the model connotations.

"fuchsia funnels breaking out"
Sight
What does this image suggest to you? What does the word "breaking" add?
Fuchsia = vivid, almost aggressive color — bold, not subtle. "Funnels" = directed, purposeful shape. "Breaking out" = forceful, like an escape or an eruption. The blossoms aren't gentle; they burst out. Spring as an act of defiance against winter.
"cotton candy-colored" / "taffy"
Taste
Both are sweet foods. Why does Limón use food comparisons for spring blossoms?
Cotton candy and taffy = fun, carnival, childhood treats. Sweet but insubstantial — you eat them and they're gone. By comparing the blossoms to junk food, Limón gently dismisses them: pretty, but not the real thing. The showy flowers are the world's "baubles and trinkets" — entertainment, not sustenance.
"confetti of aftermath"
Sight
Confetti usually signals celebration. What does "aftermath" add to that meaning?
Confetti = parties, celebrations, something festive and brief. But "aftermath" = what's left after something intense — a storm, a battle, a catastrophe. The petals on the pavement are both beautiful and a kind of wreckage. The party is over. This prepares us for the poem's turn toward something quieter and more serious.
"a green skin growing over"
Touch
Skin usually refers to the human body. What does it mean to describe leaves as "skin"?
Skin = the body's protective layer. It heals over wounds; it keeps the inside safe. By calling new leaves a "green skin growing over," Limón gives the tree a body — and suggests that the leaves are a form of healing. "Whatever winter did to us" is the wound; the leaves are the scar tissue forming. Recovery as a natural, bodily process.
"unfurling like a fist to an open palm"
Touch
A fist is closed, guarded, tense. An open palm is the opposite. What does this movement suggest?
A fist = protection, anger, resistance, self-defense. An open palm = acceptance, surrender, readiness to receive. The leaf unfurling performs the poem's entire emotional argument in a single gesture: moving from closed and guarded (winter, grief, hurt) to open and willing. "I'll take it all" — not resigned, but genuinely ready. The leaf doesn't explain or negotiate. It just opens.

Step 3 — Connections & Patterns
What do the images have in common? What larger idea connects them?
🔒
Your task

Look back across all the images you've analyzed. What patterns do you notice? Are there repeated ideas, contrasting groups, or a progression? Think it through, then reveal the model.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections
The Showy vs. The Quiet

The poem's images split into two groups. First: the loud, colorful blossoms — fuchsia, cherry, cotton candy, taffy. These are the world's "baubles and trinkets": beautiful but temporary. Second: the plain green leaves — patient, plodding, a skin growing over damage. The poem dismisses the first group and argues for the second.

A Progression: Spectacle → Ruin → Return

The images follow a seasonal and emotional arc: spring's showy blossoms → their fall (confetti, aftermath) → the quiet arrival of leaves. This mirrors a human experience: the initial burst of feeling, its collapse, and the slow return to living. "Whatever winter did to us" confirms the images carry emotional weight beyond just weather.

The Key Pattern

The taste images (cotton candy, taffy) and the touch images (skin, fist/palm) are doing different work. The taste images dismiss the showy flowers as insubstantial. The touch images locate resilience in the body — healing, opening, accepting. The poem moves from looking at spring to feeling it.

Step 4 — Interpretation
What is the poem saying? Use the images as your evidence.
🔒
Your task

Using everything you've noticed about the images, their connotations, and the patterns between them, articulate what you think this poem is saying. What is Limón arguing, and how do the images make that argument? Draft a few sentences in your head, then reveal the model interpretation.

Model Interpretation
One Strong Reading

In "Instructions on Not Giving Up," Ada Limón uses a progression of spring imagery to make an argument about quiet, unglamorous resilience. The poem opens with the season's showiest moments — fuchsia blossoms, cotton candy-colored flowers, cherry limbs — but deliberately dismisses them as "baubles and trinkets," as insubstantial as carnival candy. The real argument arrives with the plain green leaves, which the poem describes through touch images: a skin growing over a wound, a fist slowly opening into an open palm. These images locate resilience not in dramatic gesture but in the body's patient, ordinary capacity to heal and receive. The closing image — a leaf unfurling from fist to open palm — performs the poem's emotional instruction in a single gesture: stop protecting yourself from life. Take it. Take it all. Limón's poem suggests that surviving hardship isn't about triumph. It's about the decision, made quietly and repeatedly, to keep growing anyway.

A Note on Interpretation

This is one strong reading — not the only one. Your connotations may have led you somewhere different, and that's entirely valid. What matters is that your interpretation is grounded in specific images from the poem and explains how those images make meaning.

Poem Two
Terrance Hayes — "American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin"

Terrance Hayes (b. 1971) is a MacArthur Fellow and National Book Award winner. His "American Sonnets" series explores race, identity, and violence in contemporary America through formally inventive, politically charged verse.

1
Step 1 — Identify the Key Images
Read the poem carefully — it's denser than Poem 1. Identify what you can see, hear, or feel.
A note before you read

This poem is more challenging. Hayes uses street slang, proper names, and a rapid-fire associative style. Don't worry if it feels dense on first read — that's intentional. Read it twice: once straight through, once looking specifically for images. What can you picture? What can you hear?

You may also notice that many of the poem's images are digital (pixels, data, photos, clicking) rather than physical. This tension between the digital and the physical is central to the poem's meaning.

American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin — Terrance Hayes
Any day now you will have the ability to feed the name
Of anyone into an engine & your long lost half brother
As well as whoever else possesses a version of his name
Will appear before your face in bits of pixels & data
Displaying his monikers (like Gitmo for trapping, Bang
Bang for banging, Dopamine for dope or brains),
The country he would most like to visit (Heaven),
His nine & middle finger pointing towards the arms
Of the last trill trees of Bluff Estates & the arms
Of the slim fly girls the color of trees cut down & shaped
Into something a nail penetrates. I admit, right now
Technology is insufficient, but you will find them
Flashing grins & money in the photos they took
Before they were ghosts when you click here tomorrow.

Source: Poets.org

Model Answer — Key Images Identified

This poem mixes digital images (fragmented, abstract, screen-based) with physical images (bodies, trees, hands, nails). That contrast is the poem's central design. Notice also how the word "arms" appears twice — and means something different each time.

American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin — Terrance Hayes
Any day now you will have the ability to feed the name Of anyone into an engine & your long lost half brother As well as whoever else possesses a version of his name Will appear before your face in bits of pixels & data
"bits of pixels & data"
Sight — a person reduced to a digital image. Fragmented, flickering, not whole.
Displaying his monikers (like Gitmo for trapping, Bang Bang for banging, Dopamine for dope or brains), The country he would most like to visit (Heaven),
"Bang Bang" / "Gitmo" / "Dopamine"
Sound — these are street names (monikers). "Bang Bang" sounds like gunshots. "Gitmo" = Guantánamo, a name associated with imprisonment. Each nickname is a compressed story.
"Heaven"
Sight/concept — the place he most wants to visit. Aspiration or, given the poem's ending, destination.

"nine & middle finger pointing"
Sight — gesture. "Nine" = a gun (slang). Middle finger = defiance. Both fingers point. The image holds violence and resistance at once.
"arms" (twice) — "last trill trees" — "the color of trees"
Sight — "arms" = tree branches AND human arms AND firearms (a pun that holds the poem's whole argument). "Last trill trees" = trees disappearing, a neighborhood changed. Girls described as the color of trees — Black skin as natural, organic, alive.
"something a nail penetrates"
Touch — violent, piercing. Wood shaped for construction... or crucifixion. The trees (and by connection, the bodies) cut down and made into something violence enters.

"Flashing grins & money in the photos"
Sight — social media photos. The image of life at its most alive: smiling, celebrating.
"ghosts"
Sight — the dead. The word lands at the end of the poem's search-engine journey. What the algorithm finds: photos of people who are no longer alive.
2
Step 2 — Connotations
What does each image make you think of? Pay special attention to the double meanings.
🔒
Your task

Hayes is a poet of double meanings. Many of his images carry more than one connotation at once — and those layers are where the poem's argument lives. For each image, push past the surface meaning to what it suggests underneath.

"bits of pixels & data"
Sight
A person appears on a screen as pixels and data. What is lost in this kind of representation?
Pixels = tiny fragments. Data = cold, numeric. A person reduced to their digital profile loses everything that makes them human: their voice, their weight, their warmth, their history. The image suggests that technology flattens people. It promises to "find" someone, but what it finds is a representation, not a person.
"Bang Bang" as a street name
Sound
Street names often tell a story. What might "Bang Bang" say about the person who earned it — and the world that named him that?
Bang Bang = gunshots, explosions, violence. A street name earned in a world where guns are present. But it also sounds like a child's word for a toy gun — a reminder that this person was once a kid. The name encodes a life story in two words. Hayes gives us the moniker without judgment, asking us to hear what it contains rather than dismiss it.
"the last trill trees of Bluff Estates"
Sight
Why are these "the last" trees? What does "trill" (authentic/real in slang) add to the image of trees in a neighborhood?
"Last" = these trees are disappearing — the neighborhood is being changed, perhaps by gentrification or development. "Trill" = real, authentic (AAVE slang) — these are the genuinely rooted trees, the ones that belong. Bluff Estates = a specific neighborhood. The image captures a place under erasure. The trees are witnesses to what's been lost.
"trees cut down & shaped / Into something a nail penetrates"
Touch
Trees become lumber. Lumber becomes something a nail goes into. What might that "something" be — and why is the phrase left vague?
Lumber becomes houses, furniture, coffins, crosses. "Something a nail penetrates" is deliberately vague — it could be a house being built (construction) or a coffin being sealed (death) or a cross (crucifixion, sacrifice). The vagueness is the point: violence enters. The trees (already connected to the Black women described as "the color of trees") are cut down and shaped into objects violence can enter. The image holds beauty and brutality in the same breath.
"ghosts" (final word)
Sight
The poem ends with this word. How does arriving at "ghosts" after a search-engine journey change the meaning of everything before it?
Ghosts = the dead, the absent, those who haunt. The search engine promised to find these people — "any day now." It does find them: in old photos, smiling, alive. But "before they were ghosts" transforms the whole poem into an elegy. The technology works. It finds them. And what it finds is proof of who they were before they died. The algorithm cannot restore a life. It can only document its absence.

Step 3 — Connections & Patterns
What connects the digital images to the physical ones? Find the poem's central tension.
🔒
Your task

This poem has two distinct worlds of imagery: digital/abstract and physical/embodied. Look back at the images and think about how these two worlds relate to each other. What pattern do you see? Then reveal the model.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections
Digital Images — Abstract, Fragmented

"Bits of pixels & data" — "feed the name into an engine" — "photos they took" — "when you click here tomorrow." These images suggest a technological world that promises access but delivers only surfaces: screen fragments, profile data, social media photos. The digital world can find you. It cannot know you.

Physical Images — Embodied, Vulnerable

"Fingers pointing" — "trees cut down" — "the color of trees" — "something a nail penetrates" — "ghosts." These images locate real human bodies in a real landscape. They suggest vitality (alive, rooted, present) and vulnerability (cut down, penetrated, erased). The physical world is where people actually live — and die.

The Central Pattern: "Arms"

The word "arms" is the poem's hinge. It appears twice, referring to tree branches, human arms, and — by sound — firearms. Bodies and trees are connected through this pun: both are things that grow, both are things that get cut down, both are things that can be destroyed. The poem asks: what does a search engine find when it searches for a Black man? His digital profile — and behind it, evidence of a life ended by violence.

Step 4 — Interpretation
What is Hayes saying? What does the poem argue through its imagery?
🔒
Your task

You've worked through the poem's images, their connotations, and their patterns. Now put it all together: what is this poem doing? What argument does Hayes make through the imagery he chooses? Formulate your interpretation, then reveal the model.

Model Interpretation
One Strong Reading

In this "American Sonnet," Terrance Hayes uses a collision of digital and physical imagery to interrogate what technology can and cannot recover about Black lives. The poem is addressed to "you" — a reader who will use a search engine to find someone, perhaps a lost relative. The technology seems powerful: "any day now," it will deliver a person in "bits of pixels & data." But the poem's physical images — trees being cut down, bodies described as the color of wood, fingers pointing toward violence, something a nail penetrates — tell a different story. These bodies exist in a world where they are vulnerable to the same violence that fells trees. The word "arms" holds the poem's argument in a single image: tree branches, human arms, and firearms, all named the same thing, all implicated in the same cycle. The search engine finds what it finds: old photos, "grins & money," evidence of joy. But the last word — "ghosts" — transforms the poem into an elegy. Technology can document a life. It cannot explain, prevent, or restore one.

A Note on Interpretation

Hayes's poem is deliberately challenging and resists any single reading. If your interpretation moved in a different direction — focusing on family, surveillance, identity, or grief — that's equally valid, as long as you can point to specific images as evidence. The goal of poetry analysis is not to find the meaning, but to make a defensible one.
