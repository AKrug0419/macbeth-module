📄 Primary Sources for ENC 1102

Primary sources look different in each course you take — they're determined largely by the academic subject you're studying. For ENC 1102, primary sources are the works of literature you've studied this semester. Any work of art — a painting, photograph, film, song, poem, or short story — is considered a primary source in the field of literary or cultural studies.

Remember all of the quotes you included in the body paragraphs of your Literary Analysis Essay? Without knowing it, you were already quoting from a primary source. Your study question responses for each short story this semester? Also built around primary source evidence.

For your Literary Research Essay, your primary source is the short story you've selected to write about. You'll be quoting from it directly in each body paragraph — just as you did in your first essay, but now combined with secondary source evidence as well.

🔍 Secondary Sources for ENC 1102

For ENC 1102, secondary sources are essays and articles written by literary scholars — professors and published critics with expertise in literature and literary analysis. In their articles, they explain, analyze, or interpret the short story you're writing about. You'll use the insights and arguments of these experts as additional evidence to support your thesis and body paragraphs.

Unlike your first essay, you'll now be quoting not only from a primary source (the short story itself) but also from three secondary sources.

📌
What Counts as a Valid Secondary Source for This Essay

The scope of acceptable secondary sources is intentionally narrow. Your three secondary sources must be literary criticism focused specifically on the short story you are analyzing — scholarly articles that interpret, analyze, or argue something about the themes, characters, symbols, or narrative techniques of your chosen story.

✅ Acceptable Secondary Sources
  • A scholarly article analyzing the themes, characters, or symbols of your chosen short story
  • An academic essay interpreting the narrative structure or literary techniques of your chosen story
  • A chapter from a literary criticism book focused on your story or its author
❌ Not Acceptable for This Essay
  • Biographical articles about the author's life that don't analyze the story itself
  • Historical or cultural context essays not focused on literary analysis of your specific story
  • General encyclopedia or summary articles (Wikipedia, SparkNotes, Cliffs Notes)
  • Sources about a different work by the same author

🔖 What's New About This Essay

Your first essay this semester required only primary source quotes — you quoted from the story and analyzed those quotes yourself. Your Literary Research Essay adds a second layer: in addition to quoting the story, you'll also be bringing in what scholars have argued about it, and then using your own analysis to connect those scholarly insights to your argument. The essay still centers on your own argument — but now it's supported by both kinds of evidence.
