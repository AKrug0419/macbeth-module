Section 2 — Quoting from Your Secondary Sources

Your three secondary sources may be PDF articles (with page numbers) or HTML articles (no page numbers). The citation format changes depending on the source type and where the author’s name appears. There are four patterns — learn them all.

Part One — Core Skills
1  —  Signal Phrases for Secondary Sources

A signal phrase introduces a quotation by naming the author or providing context. For secondary sources, strong signal phrases establish the scholar’s identity and authority. It is generally preferable to name the scholar in the signal phrase rather than hiding their name inside the parenthetical — this gives the scholar credit and makes the sentence read more naturally. Alternate between placing the name in the signal phrase and placing it in the parenthetical so that your paragraphs do not become repetitive.

Signal verbs for secondary sources — vary these throughout your essay:
argues • claims • contends • maintains • suggests • observes • notes • explains • writes • points out • concludes • asserts • demonstrates • shows

⚠️ Always use present tense for signal verbs with literary criticism: “Boyd argues” not “Boyd argued.” Scholars’ arguments remain active in the present, even if the article was published decades ago.

✓  Author Introduced by Name and Role

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112).

Naming the scholar and identifying their field establishes their authority and makes the source feel purposeful.

✓  Author Name in Parenthetical Instead

Scholars of Hurston have noted that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (Boyd 112).

Name omitted from signal phrase → must appear in the parenthetical citation before the page number.

✓  PDF Source: Identifying Scholar’s Field

Folklore scholar Patricia Yaeger notes that the washtub in “Sweat” “operates as both a literal instrument of labor and a symbolic marker of Delia’s territorial claim to the house she has earned” (78).

Identifying the scholar’s field signals why their perspective is relevant to your argument.

2  —  Pattern A: PDF Article — Author’s Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is a PDF article and you have not named the author in the signal phrase, include both the author’s last name and the page number inside the parenthetical citation. No comma separates them. The period falls after the closing parenthesis.

Format
“quotation” (LastName PageNumber).   →   e.g., (Boyd 112) or (Day 34)
✗  Comma Between Name and Page Number

Scholars of Hurston have noted that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd, 112).

Never place a comma between the author’s name and the page number in MLA format.

✓  Correct — No Comma

Scholars of Hurston have noted that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd 112).

✓  Two-Author Source

Critics have argued that Carter’s fairy-tale revisions “dismantle the passive-female archetype from within the very genre that produced it” (Warner and Zipes 45).

Two-author source: use both last names connected with “and.”

3  —  Pattern B: PDF Article — Author’s Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is a PDF article and you have named the author in the signal phrase, the parenthetical citation contains the page number only — the author’s name is not repeated.

Format
Author argues that “quotation” (PageNumber).   →   e.g., Boyd argues that “…” (112).
✗  Name Repeated in Citation

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd 112).

Author named in signal phrase → do not repeat the name in the citation. Page number only.

✓  Correct — Page Number Only

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (112).

✓  Another Example

Carter scholar Patricia Day notes that the wolf-bridegroom “is not defeated but domesticated, and domestication in Carter is always a mutual transformation” (34).

Author named in signal phrase → page number only in citation.

4  —  Pattern C: HTML Article — Author’s Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is an HTML article (no fixed page numbers) and you have not named the author in the signal phrase, include only the author’s last name in the parenthetical citation. There is no page number — do not invent or estimate one.

Format
“quotation” (LastName).   →   e.g., (Friedman) or (Pinsker)
✗  Invented Page Number for HTML Source

Scholars have observed that Ozzie’s rooftop stand “converts an act of rebellion into a demand for moral accountability” (Pinsker 7).

HTML articles have no fixed pages. Do not invent or estimate a page number.

✓  Correct — Author Only, No Page Number

Scholars have observed that Ozzie’s rooftop stand “converts an act of rebellion into a demand for moral accountability” (Pinsker).

✓  Another HTML Example

Critics of Carter’s fiction have noted that the girl in the story “refuses the script of victimhood at precisely the moment the genre demands she accept it” (Makinen).

HTML source → last name only in parenthetical; no page number.

5  —  Pattern D: HTML Article — Author’s Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is an HTML article and you have named the author in the signal phrase, no parenthetical citation is needed at all. Simply end the sentence with a period after the closing quotation mark.

Format
Author argues that “quotation.”   →   No parenthetical needed. Period goes inside the closing quotation mark.
⚠️ Important Note on Punctuation

When there is no parenthetical citation, American English convention places the period inside the closing quotation mark: “quotation.” This is the one situation where a period appears inside the quotation marks. When a parenthetical citation follows, the period always goes after the closing parenthesis: “quotation” (Author Page).

✗  Unnecessary Citation Added

Roth scholar Sanford Pinsker has argued that the rooftop scene “converts an act of rebellion into a demand for moral accountability” (Pinsker).

Author already named in signal phrase → the parenthetical citation is redundant. Remove it.

✓  Correct — No Parenthetical Needed

Roth scholar Sanford Pinsker has argued that the rooftop scene “converts an act of rebellion into a demand for moral accountability.”

✓  Another HTML + Signal Phrase Example

Carter scholar Merja Makinen has noted that the girl’s decision to stay with the wolf “rewrites the terms of the fairy tale from a position of desire rather than submission.”

HTML source + author in signal phrase = no parenthetical citation needed.

6  —  Articles With No Identified Author

Occasionally, an article in an academic database will not list a named author. In that case, use the first one to three significant words of the article’s title inside the parenthetical citation, in quotation marks. These sources are relatively rare in ENC 1102, and it is generally preferable to choose sources with identifiable authors — named scholars carry more scholarly weight. If your source is a PDF and has a page number, include the page number after the title fragment.

Format
“quotation” (“First Words”)  ←  HTML   |   “quotation” (“First Words” PageNumber)  ←  PDF
✓  HTML Source — No Author

One overview of Hurston’s fiction notes that her characters consistently “claim physical space as a precondition for claiming psychic freedom” (“Overview of Hurston”).

No author identified → use first significant words of the title. HTML article: no page number.

✓  PDF Source — No Author (Page Number Included)

Critics have noted that Carter uses the fairy-tale frame “as a deliberate site of ideological contestation rather than simple entertainment” (“Carter’s Feminist” 22).

PDF article with no author → title fragment in quotation marks, followed by the page number.

⚠️ The most common error is repeating the author’s name in both the signal phrase and the citation. Pick one location for the name and use it there only. See Section 11 below.

Part Two — Common Quoting Errors
7  —  Isolated / Floating Quotes Common Error

A floating quote is a quotation that stands alone as its own sentence without being introduced or integrated into your own prose. Every secondary source quotation must be attached to your own words before and after: a signal phrase establishes who is speaking and why their words matter, and your own analysis explains what the evidence contributes to your argument.

✗  Floating Quote

Delia’s labor defines her identity. “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (Boyd 112). O’Connor uses this to show her strength.

The quote stands alone with no signal phrase and only a vague sentence following it.

✓  Quote Introduced and Followed by Analysis

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112). Boyd’s reading clarifies what the washtub passages establish: the house and the labor that keeps it are Delia’s only measure of independence, which is precisely why Sykes targets them.

💡  The Signal-Quote-Analysis Pattern

Every secondary source use follows the same three-part structure: (1) a signal phrase that names the scholar and establishes context, (2) the quoted or paraphrased material, and (3) your own sentence or two of analysis connecting the scholar’s point to your argument. The analysis is not optional.

8  —  Comma Splices Involving Quotes Common Error

A common quoting error is using a comma to join your own complete independent clause to a quotation. If your lead-in is a complete sentence, use a colon, not a comma. Alternatively, restructure the lead-in so the quotation flows naturally from an incomplete construction — usually more elegant.

✗  Comma Between Complete Clause and Quote

Boyd makes an important point about this scene, “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (112).

“Boyd makes an important point” is a complete sentence — a comma cannot join it to the quotation.

✓  Colon After Complete Lead-In

Boyd makes an important point about this scene: “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (112).

✓  Restructure So Quote Flows From Incomplete Lead-In

Boyd connects labor to identity, arguing that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (112).

“By arguing that” is incomplete — the quote flows naturally; no colon needed.

9  —  Colon Before a Quote — Signal Phrase Must Be a Complete Sentence Common Error

A colon may introduce a quotation only when the signal phrase is a complete sentence. If your lead-in is an incomplete construction (such as “Boyd notes that” or “According to Day”), do not use a colon; let the quotation flow directly.

✗  Colon After Incomplete Lead-In

Day notes that: “the wolf-bridegroom is not defeated but domesticated, and domestication in Carter is always a mutual transformation” (34).

“Day notes that” is not a complete sentence — a colon cannot follow it.

✓  No Colon: Quote Flows Naturally

Day notes that “the wolf-bridegroom is not defeated but domesticated, and domestication in Carter is always a mutual transformation” (34).

✓  Colon Correct After a Complete Sentence

Day identifies the story’s central paradox: “the wolf-bridegroom is not defeated but domesticated, and domestication in Carter is always a mutual transformation” (34).

“Day identifies the story’s central paradox” is a complete sentence — the colon is correct.

10  —  Punctuation Left Inside the Quotation Marks Common Error

When a quotation is followed by a parenthetical citation, do not leave the original text’s ending punctuation inside the closing quotation mark. The exceptions are question marks and exclamation marks that belong to the quoted material itself; these stay inside.

✗  Original Period Left Inside

Boyd argues that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase.” (112).

The period from the original text should not appear here — this also creates double punctuation.

✓  Original Period Removed

Boyd argues that Delia’s labor “is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112).

✓  Exception: Question Mark Stays Inside

Pinsker poses a central question about the story’s ending: “Does Ozzie’s rooftop triumph actually change anyone below — or does the crowd simply go home?” (Pinsker).

Question marks from the original text stay inside. The period after the citation still closes your sentence.

11  —  Author’s Name Appearing in Both Signal Phrase and Citation Common Error

The author’s name belongs in one place only: either in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation — never in both. When the name appears in the signal phrase, the citation contains only the page number (PDF) or nothing (HTML). Repeating the name in both places is redundant and incorrect.

✗  Name Repeated in Signal Phrase and Citation

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd 112).

Author named in signal phrase → remove the name from the citation. Page number only: (112).

✓  Correct — Name in Signal Phrase Only

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (112).

✓  Correct — Name in Citation Only

One critic connects this moment to Hurston’s broader feminist argument, arguing that “Delia’s labor is not merely economic survival but a form of self-authorship” (Boyd 112).

No name in signal phrase → last name must appear in the citation.

12  —  Missing End Punctuation After a Quote Common Error

When a quotation ends your sentence, the sentence still needs its own closing punctuation — a period, semicolon, or colon — placed after the parenthetical citation. When there is no parenthetical citation (HTML source, author in signal phrase), the period goes inside the closing quotation mark instead.

✗  Missing Period After Citation

Boyd argues that labor represents “a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112)

The sentence ends after the closing parenthesis with no period.

✓  Correct — Period After Citation

Boyd argues that labor represents “a form of self-authorship that Sykes systematically attempts to erase” (112).

✓  Semicolon After Citation: Joining Two Clauses

Day argues that the wolf-groom “is not defeated but domesticated” (34); this framing explains why the ending refuses simple triumph and insists on something more unsettling.

A semicolon after the citation is correct when joining two independent clauses.
