Orientation

Building on Poetic Imagery

In the previous lesson, you learned to identify the concrete images that make a poem vivid — the sensory details that paint pictures and activate the senses. Figurative language builds directly on that foundation. It incorporates everything you already know about imagery, but adds a single, transformative element: comparison.

When a poet compares one thing to another — especially two things that seem to have nothing in common — something surprising happens. The comparison doesn't just describe; it reveals. It allows a poet to communicate a precise, personal perspective on any idea, emotion, or experience that plain language simply can't achieve.

Three Types, One Core Idea

All three types of figurative language we'll study — metaphor, simile, and personification — involve comparison. They differ only in how that comparison is made: implied, explicit with signal words, or directed specifically at human qualities.

Technique Definition Signal Example
Metaphor An implied comparison between two unlike things — one is said to be the other No signal words — the comparison is embedded "His voice was a well of dark water"
Simile An explicit comparison between two unlike things using "like" or "as" like  or  as "Her voice rose like a balloon"
Personification A metaphor in which a non-human thing is given human qualities or actions Unexpected human verbs or attributes on non-human subjects "The wind whispered its secret through the pines"

Technique Overview

What Is Figurative Language?

Oregon State University — Figurative Language
✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1What is figurative language, and how does it differ from literal language?
Answer

Figurative language uses words beyond their literal meanings to create comparisons, associations, and vivid images. Literal language says exactly what it means — "the room was cold" is literal. Figurative language creates meaning indirectly: "the room breathed ice" uses comparison and personification to communicate not just temperature but feeling. Poets rely on figurative language because it can carry more emotional and intellectual weight than direct statement.

2Why do poets rely on figurative language rather than simply stating their ideas directly?
Answer

Because comparison is uniquely precise. When a poet says a father's voice is "a well of dark water," the image carries connotations — depth, coolness, something to draw from, something you can't see the bottom of — that no single adjective can replicate. Figurative language communicates a poet's specific perspective on an experience, not just a description of it.

3What three types of figurative language will this unit focus on, and what do they have in common?
Answer

Metaphor, simile, and personification. All three are fundamentally about comparison — connecting two unlike things to generate new meaning. They differ in method: similes signal their comparison with "like" or "as"; metaphors keep the comparison implicit; and personification specifically compares a non-human thing to a person or human experience.

Every act of figurative language asks the reader to hold two things in mind at once — the thing being described (what literary critics call the tenor) and the thing it's being compared to (the vehicle). The power of the comparison comes from the gap between them: the more unlike the two things are, the more the comparison can surprise, illuminate, and reframe.

A Note on Connotation

When you encounter a figurative comparison in a poem, your analysis task is the same as it was with plain images: ask what each element connotes. Both the tenor and the vehicle bring their own associations into the comparison. Your job is to figure out which connotations are being activated and why.

Technique Two
Metaphor

The Implied Comparison

Oregon State University — Metaphors
✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1What is a metaphor, and what makes it different from a simile?
Answer

A metaphor is a direct but implied comparison between two unlike things — it states or implies that one thing is another, without using "like" or "as." A simile announces its comparison explicitly with those signal words. Metaphors feel more seamless and embedded in the poem's language, which is part of why poets frequently prefer them to similes.

2The video suggests that unexpected verbs are often a clue to finding metaphors. Why?
Answer

When a verb doesn't literally apply to its subject — when "breath flickers" or "nakedness shadows" — it signals a hidden comparison. The subject is being implicitly compared to something that does perform that action. An unexpected verb is often the seam where two unlike things are stitched together in a metaphor. It's a practical thing to look for when reading unfamiliar poems.

3What is personification, and how does it relate to metaphor?
Answer

Personification is a special kind of metaphor in which a non-human thing — an object, animal, force of nature, or abstraction — is given specifically human qualities or behaviors. Every personification is a metaphor, but not every metaphor is personification. The comparison in personification is always between the non-human subject and a person or human experience.

A regular metaphor works by identification: it declares or implies that X is Y, and the reader's task is to figure out what qualities of Y the poet wants us to transfer onto X. The comparison is never random — it's calibrated to illuminate something specific about the tenor.

Personification is the most emotionally charged variety. By giving a non-human thing human will, voice, or agency, a poet makes it capable of intention — which makes it capable of being frightening, comforting, or meaningful in ways that inert objects simply cannot be.

The Verb as Clue

When you can't find a metaphor on the first read, look for verbs that seem off — actions a thing doesn't normally perform. Ask: what kind of thing does do that? The answer is the implied comparison. This is one of the most reliable practical skills for identifying metaphors in unfamiliar poems.

Technique Three
Simile

The Explicit Comparison

Oregon State University — Similes
✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1What are the two signal words that identify a simile, and why do they matter?
Answer

"Like" and "as." These words signal that a comparison is being made between two unlike things. They're important because they make the simile explicit — the poet is openly inviting you to see the connection. When you spot either word, your job is to identify the two things being compared and ask what the comparison reveals.

2A simile creates two images in a single line. What challenge does this create for the reader?
Answer

The reader must engage with both images — not just the thing being described, but also what it's being compared to — and figure out what qualities are being transferred from one to the other. Each half of the simile carries its own connotations. This is double the analytical work of a plain image, but also double the potential for meaning.

3If similes are easier to identify than metaphors, why might a poet sometimes prefer a regular metaphor instead?
Answer

Metaphors integrate more smoothly into a poem's language — they don't pause the line to announce themselves the way "like" or "as" does. Overusing similes can make writing feel labored or tentative, as if the poet isn't quite committing to the comparison. A metaphor that simply declares one thing to be another often feels bolder, more intimate, and more certain.

Because similes announce themselves, they're the most accessible entry point into figurative language — and an excellent place to start when analyzing an unfamiliar poem. Once you've located the similes, you've identified at least some of the poem's key comparisons, which gives you a foundation for the harder work of finding the implied metaphors.

Engaging Both Images

Never stop at the simile's first image. If Plath writes that her baby's cry is like "a handful of notes" and then "clear vowels rise like balloons," you must ask what balloons connote — lightness, rising, celebration, childhood — and how those connotations transfer to the baby's voice. Both halves of every simile are doing work.

Technique Four
Personification

The Human Projected onto the World

Oregon State University — Personification
✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1What is personification, and how does it relate to metaphor?
Answer

Personification is a figure of speech in which a non-human thing — an object, animal, abstract concept, or force of nature — is given specifically human qualities, behaviors, or emotions. It is a subtype of metaphor: every personification is an implied comparison between a non-human subject and a person or human experience. All personifications are metaphors, but not all metaphors are personifications — the distinction is the specific target of comparison: humanity.

2Why do poets use personification — what does it allow them to express that plain description cannot?
Answer

Personification makes the non-human world capable of intention — and intention creates meaning. A wind that simply blows is a weather event. A wind that whispers, or mourns, or threatens, is suddenly capable of relationship with a human speaker. Personification allows poets to project emotion onto the outer world, suggesting that nature, objects, or abstract forces are somehow aligned with — or indifferent to — human experience. It collapses the boundary between the inner world and the outer one.

3How can you recognize personification when reading an unfamiliar poem?
Answer

Look for non-human subjects performing characteristically human actions: objects that whisper, remember, wait, or decide; natural forces that grieve or threaten; abstractions that visit, speak, or abandon. When you find one, ask: what specific human quality has been assigned, and why that quality? The particular human behavior the poet chooses is always purposeful — it tells you exactly what relationship the poem is proposing between the human speaker and the non-human world.

Personification is the most emotionally charged variety of figurative language precisely because it makes the non-human world seem to have skin in the game. By giving an object or abstraction human will, voice, or feeling, the poet suggests that the outer world and the inner world are in some way continuous — that grief, for instance, isn't merely inside us but radiates outward, shapes the landscape, inhabits the weather.

What Does It Make the Non-Human Do?

When you identify a personification, don't stop at naming it. Ask what specific human quality has been assigned — and why that quality rather than another. A pool that "waits" is very different from a pool that "mourns" or "watches." The specific human behavior the poet assigns is always meaningful: it tells you something precise about the poem's emotional and thematic stakes.

Example One

Li-Young Lee — "The Gift"

Li-Young Lee (b. 1957) is known for poetry that transforms intimate family moments into meditations on love, memory, and inherited tenderness. "The Gift" recounts a father removing a metal splinter from his son's palm — and layers that small act with an extraordinary density of figurative language. Notice how Lee uses all three techniques, often within a few lines of each other.

The Gift — Li-Young Lee
To pull the metal splinter from my palm my father recited a story in a low voice. I watched his lovely face and not the blade. Before the story ended, he'd removed the iron sliver I thought I'd die from.
I can't remember the tale, but hear his voice still, a well of dark water, a prayer. And I recall his hands, two measures of tenderness he laid against my face, the flames of discipline he raised above my head.
Metaphor
"a well of dark water, a prayer"
Three metaphors for the father's voice in rapid succession. A well suggests depth, something to draw from, something cool and ancient. "Dark water" adds mystery — you can't see the bottom. "A prayer" adds reverence. Together, they make the voice feel nourishing and sacred, not just soothing.
Metaphor
"two measures of tenderness" / "the flames of discipline"
The father's hands carry two metaphors — one for love, one for authority. "Measures" suggests precision, care, something carefully apportioned. "Flames" suggests intensity and heat, but also transformation. The paradox is the point: the same hands that discipline are the hands that heal.
Had you entered that afternoon you would have thought you saw a man planting something in a boy's palm, a silver tear, a tiny flame.
Metaphor
"a silver tear, a tiny flame"
The splinter — a mundane piece of metal — is transformed into two charged images. A tear suggests pain and grief; a flame suggests danger but also illumination. Both metaphors anticipate the personification to come: the splinter is already being given emotional and violent significance before the speaker imagines it as a conscious killer.
Had you followed that boy you would have arrived here, where I bend over my wife's right hand. Look how I shave her thumbnail down so carefully she feels no pain. Watch as I lift the splinter out. I was seven when my father took my hand like this,
Simile
"took my hand like this"
A quiet, powerful simile connecting past and present. The comparison is between two moments across time — the father's gesture then and the speaker's gesture now. "Like this" collapses the distance between them, suggesting that love and care are passed down through imitation as much as through memory. The simile is the hinge the entire poem turns on.
and I did not hold that shard between my fingers and think, Metal that will bury me, christen it Little Assassin, Ore Going Deep for My Heart. And I did not lift up my wound and cry, Death visited here!
Personification
"Metal that will bury me" / "Little Assassin" / "Ore Going Deep for My Heart"
The speaker imagines — and explicitly rejects — a series of personifications in which the splinter becomes a murderous agent with will, intention, and lethal purpose. By refusing these personifications, the poem argues that the same object can be a gift or a weapon depending on how it's received. His father taught him to receive pain as tenderness.
I did what a child does when he's given something to keep. I kissed my father.

Example Two

Jorie Graham — "Salmon"

Jorie Graham (b. 1950) is one of the most celebrated American poets writing today, known for dense, philosophically rich work that pushes language to its limits. "Salmon" (Erosion, 1983) opens with a memory of watching salmon on television — and transforms that banal image into an extended meditation on drive, desire, and what compels living things to move. Where Lee's poem is intimate and familial, Graham's is expansive and philosophical. Notice how her metaphors for the salmon gradually come to describe human sexuality itself, revealing that the poem was never simply about fish. Note also that this poem leans heavily on metaphor and personification — a useful reminder that not every poem deploys all three techniques equally.

Salmon — Jorie Graham
I watched them once, at dusk, on television, run, in our motel room half-way through Nebraska, quick, glittering, past beauty, past the importance of beauty, archaic, not even hungry, not even endangered, driving deeper and deeper into less. They leapt up falls, ladders, and rock, tearing and leaping, a gold river, and a blue river traveling in opposite directions.
Metaphor
"a gold river, / and a blue river traveling / in opposite directions"
The salmon are not described as being like rivers — they are rivers. Rivers are massive, unstoppable, ancient; they move as a collective body rather than as individuals. By declaring the salmon to be rivers, Graham transforms a TV image of fish into something geological and elemental. Notice the colors: "gold" and "blue" — not salmon-pink, but metal and sky. The colors carry connotations of value and depth rather than biology. These color-metaphors will return late in the poem.
They would not stop, resolution of will and helplessness, as the eye is helpless when the image forms itself, upside-down, backward, driving up into the mind, and the world unfastens itself from the deep ocean of the given. . . . Justice, aspen leaves, mother attempting suicide, the white night-flying moth the ants dismantled bit by bit and carried in right through the crack in my wall. . . . How helpless the still pool is, upstream, awaiting the gold blade of their hurry.
Personification
"resolution of will / and helplessness" / "the world / unfastens itself"
Will and helplessness are purely human psychological states — fish don't consciously "resolve" or experience helplessness as an internal condition. By assigning these states to the salmon, Graham makes them into agents of desire: things that want and are also compelled. This tension between will and helplessness is the poem's central philosophical subject: the overlap between intention and biological drive. Two lines later, the world itself is personified — given the agency to "unfasten itself" — as if watching the salmon destabilizes ordinary reality.
Metaphor
"awaiting the gold blade / of their hurry"
Two figures fused in two lines. The pool "awaiting" is personification — still water doesn't wait or anticipate. By giving the pool expectation, Graham creates a landscape that is breathlessly watching the salmon. The salmon's motion, meanwhile, becomes "the gold blade of their hurry" — a metaphor capturing both their speed (a blade cuts) and their collective glow (gold, echoing the river metaphor). The blade is precise, dangerous, and beautiful all at once.
Once, indoors, a child, I watched, at noon, through slatted wooden blinds, a man and woman, naked, eyes closed, climb onto each other, on the terrace floor, and ride—two gold currents wrapping round and round each other, fastening, unfastening. I hardly knew what I saw. Whatever shadow there was in that world it was the one each cast onto the other, the thin black seam they seemed to be trying to work away between them. I held my breath. As far as I could tell, the work they did with sweat and light was good. I’d say they traveled far in opposite directions. What is the light at the end of the day, deep, reddish-gold, bathing the walls, the corridors, light that is no longer light, no longer clarifies, illuminates, antique, freed from the body of the air that carries it. What is it for the space of time where it is useless, merely beautiful? When they were done, they made a distance one from the other and slept, outstretched, on the warm tile of the terrace floor, smiling, faces pressed against the stone.

Metaphor
"two gold currents / wrapping round and round each other"
This is the poem's structural keystone. Graham has spent the first half comparing salmon to gold rivers; now she describes two humans as "gold currents" doing exactly what the salmon were doing — traveling, wrapping, fastening and unfastening, moving in opposite directions. The metaphor functions as a bridge: the salmon and the humans are revealed to be versions of the same thing — organisms driven by the same fundamental compulsion. What we call "desire" in humans and what we call "instinct" in fish are, Graham suggests, one force. Her figurative language has been building this argument from the first line.
