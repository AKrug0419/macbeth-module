📝 Essay Topics — Select One

Select one of the three topics below. Your essay must be about the story listed for your chosen topic — no substitutions. If you wrote your first essay about one of these stories, select a different story for this assignment; you may not re-use your earlier essay.

Topic One — Literary Characters & Conflicts
Philip Roth — "The Conversion of the Jews"

Analyze Ozzie's internal and external conflicts and how he teaches his mother and rabbi an important lesson at the end of the story.

First, identify and explain Ozzie's internal, philosophical conflict over the issue of Mary's conceiving of Jesus. Address Judaism's, Christianity's, and Ozzie's own position on this topic (via the story's opening scene with Itzie).

Next, identify and explain Ozzie's external conflicts with his mother and Rabbi. Both physically strike Ozzie — is their behavior justified? Why?

Finally, carefully interpret the story's rooftop ending where Ozzie metaphorically "converts" everyone. What might Ozzie's real purpose be in what he tells his mother and rabbi?

📖 Include 2–3 brief, direct quotes from the story in each body paragraph.
Conclusion direction: What does this story suggest about religious authority, adolescent rebellion, or moral courage?
Topic Two — Literary Symbols
Zora Neale Hurston — "Sweat"

Analyze the symbolism in the story to argue that Sykes's domestic violence and emotional abuse represent a systematic attempt to erase Delia's autonomy — and that the story's resolution restores it.

First, analyze how Hurston uses the washtub (and Delia's labor more broadly) to establish Delia's economic independence and moral authority within the household. What does Sykes's effort to take it from her symbolize?

Next, analyze the symbolic function of the snake in the story. Trace how Sykes uses it as an instrument of terror, and argue what its ultimate role in the story's conclusion reveals about power and its limits.

Finally, analyze the ending of the story. What does Delia's silence as Sykes dies suggest about her moral transformation over the course of the narrative?

📖 Include 2–3 brief, direct quotes from the story in each body paragraph.
Conclusion direction: What does this story suggest about domestic power, self-determination, or moral justice?
Topic Three — Literary Characters & Conflicts
Angela Carter — "The Company of Wolves"

Analyze how Carter's protagonist subverts the expected gender roles of the fairy tale tradition to argue that the story is ultimately about a young woman's intelligence and self-determination.

First, examine Carter's characterization of the girl. How does she differ from the typical fairy tale heroine? What specific details establish her as active, intelligent, and self-possessed rather than passive and fearful?

Next, analyze the story's climactic confrontation. Rather than being victimized, the girl takes control of the encounter. What specific choices does she make, and what do they reveal about her character?

Finally, interpret the story's ending. What does the girl's position at the close of the story — sleeping peacefully in the wolf's arms — suggest about sexuality, power, and self-determination?

📖 Include 2–3 brief, direct quotes from the story in each body paragraph.
Conclusion direction: What does this story suggest about female agency, sexuality, or the power of intelligence?

Evidence Requirements

📖 Quotes from the Primary Source

You should have 2–3 quotes per body paragraph from the short story you're writing about.

🔍 Quotes from the Secondary Sources

Total: Six quotes from your three secondary sources combined — not six from each article. Types: Four direct quotes and two paraphrases.

📚 Works Cited

All three secondary sources and your primary source must be included on your Works Cited page — four entries total. See the The Works Cited page and the sample student research essay for formatting guidance.

⚖️ Academic Integrity

Valencia College is dedicated to maintaining an academic environment consistent with high standards including a shared commitment to transparency, integrity, and creating the right conditions for learning. All work is expected to reflect individual knowledge, thought, research, effort, and self-expression — or to properly cite the origin of the work. Valencia Policy 6Hx28:8-11 ↗

Before submitting, ask yourself:

Is this essay entirely in your own words, except for properly cited quotations and paraphrases?
Is this essay your own original work — not submitted, or based on work submitted, for another class?
Was AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) used to write, rewrite, outline, or substantially assist in drafting any part of this essay? (If yes: this is a violation.)
Were online translators used to translate the essay from another language into English? (Also a violation.)
Are you prepared to redo the essay, in part or in whole, if Canvas's AI Report flags AI-influenced content?

Violations may result in a zero or having to redo the assignment. Students have 7 days to respond to any allegation. See the Academic Integrity page for full details on consequences.
