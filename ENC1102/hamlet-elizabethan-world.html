The Age

A World of Extraordinary Confidence

Elizabeth I ascended the English throne in 1558 and ruled for forty-four years — long enough to give her name to an entire culture. The Elizabethan age was a period of imperial expansion, Protestant consolidation, devastating plague, brilliant artistic production, and extraordinary political instability, all compressed into a single reign held together by the sheer force of one woman's personality and the machinery of a court that never stopped performing.

It was also a world in profound anxiety about its own future. Elizabeth refused to name a successor. Every year she aged without an heir was a year England teetered on the edge of the succession crisis that had torn it apart before. The Elizabethan obsession with mortality, legitimacy, and the fragility of power was not philosophical — it was daily political reality. Shakespeare did not need to invent Hamlet's world. He was living in it.

Context

Six Pillars of the Elizabethan World

To understand what Shakespeare's audiences brought to the theater, you need to understand the intellectual and political furniture of their world. Six ideas shaped how an Elizabethan Londoner made sense of everything they saw on stage.

I

The Great Chain of Being

The Elizabethan cosmos was hierarchical and ordained — God at the apex, then angels, then monarchs, then nobles, then commoners, then animals, then plants, then stones. Every creature had its proper place. To violate that order — to kill a king, to marry above your station, to usurp a throne — was not merely a crime. It was a rupture in the fabric of creation itself.

II

Protestant England

Henry VIII's break with Rome had made England officially Protestant, but the Reformation was still raw, contested, and violent. Catholics were executed. Theological questions — about purgatory, about the nature of the soul, about what happened to the dead — were not academic. They were matters of state, identity, and survival. Hamlet's ghost exists in this charged theological atmosphere.

III

Humanism & the New Learning

The Renaissance had arrived in England via Italy and the Low Countries, bringing with it a new confidence in human reason, classical learning, and the individual mind's capacity to understand the world. Hamlet's education at Wittenberg — the university of Luther — marks him as a product of this new intellectual culture, in violent collision with the feudal blood-obligation world he has inherited.

IV

Plague & Mortality

Bubonic plague killed tens of thousands of Londoners during Elizabeth's reign, closing the theaters repeatedly. Death was not an abstraction in this world — it was a neighbor. The Elizabethan preoccupation with mortality, corruption, and the skull beneath the skin was not morbid posturing. It was an honest reckoning with lived experience. Hamlet's graveyard scene was not shocking. It was familiar.

V

Succession & Political Anxiety

Elizabeth's refusal to name an heir created a permanent low-grade political crisis. What happens when the monarch dies without succession? Who has the legitimate claim? How is power transferred — and at what cost? These were not hypothetical questions. They were the urgent political concerns of every educated Elizabethan. They are also, precisely, the questions Hamlet is built around.

VI

Theater as Public Life

The theater in Elizabethan London was not a high-culture institution for educated elites. It was a popular entertainment attended by everyone — apprentices, merchants, courtiers, prostitutes, lawyers, servants — standing together in the yard or seated in the galleries. Shakespeare wrote for all of them simultaneously, which is why Hamlet operates as both a revenge thriller and a philosophical meditation, often in the same speech.

The Theatre

The Globe & the World It Made

The Globe Theatre was built in 1599 on the South Bank of the Thames — outside the jurisdiction of the City of London, which regarded theatrical performances with deep suspicion. It was constructed partly from the timber of an earlier playhouse, The Theatre, which Shakespeare's company had dismantled and carried across the river. The Globe was, from its first stone, an act of defiance and ambition.

It held approximately three thousand people. The yard — standing room, open to the sky — cost a penny. The galleries, covered and seated, cost more. Rain fell on the groundlings; the stage thrust into the yard so that the actors performed surrounded on three sides by the audience. There was no artificial lighting: performances happened in the afternoon, in full daylight, and the audience could see one another as clearly as they could see the stage.

This architecture shaped everything Shakespeare wrote. Characters spoke directly to the audience — not "to themselves" in a theatrical convention, but to the three thousand people standing in the yard. Hamlet's soliloquies were not private. They were public confessions made to a crowd. This changes what they mean.

The South Bank

The Globe shared its neighborhood with bear-baiting arenas, brothels, and taverns. The South Bank was London's entertainment district precisely because it was beyond the city's moral jurisdiction. Theater and transgression were neighbors by design. The plays performed at the Globe existed in this context — popular, commercial, slightly illicit, and absolutely central to the culture.

The Lord Chamberlain's Men

Shakespeare's company — the Lord Chamberlain's Men, renamed the King's Men after James I took the throne in 1603 — was the preeminent theatrical company in London. Shakespeare was not merely an employee; he was a shareholder. His financial stake in the company meant he had direct incentive to write plays that filled the Globe's yard three thousand people at a time. Hamlet was a commercial product as well as a work of art.

The company's leading tragedian was Richard Burbage — the first actor to play Hamlet, and the man for whose specific physical and vocal presence Shakespeare wrote the role.

Performance & the Living Text

Elizabethan theatrical performance was not the reverent encounter with a fixed, sacred text that modern productions sometimes suggest. Plays were cut, adapted, performed differently for different audiences (the Globe's yard versus a private court performance), and revised continuously. The text of Hamlet that has come down to us exists in three different versions — none of them definitive, all of them evidence of a living performance tradition.

Shakespeare wrote for actors he knew, in a building he co-owned, for audiences he could see from the stage.

The Man

Shakespeare in His World

The Chandos Portrait Attributed to John Taylor · c. 1600–1610 The most likely contemporary portrait of Shakespeare
The Chandos Portrait · attr. John Taylor · c. 1600

William Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564, the son of a glover and alderman. He received a grammar school education — Latin, rhetoric, classical literature — that was standard for the son of a respectable tradesman. He did not attend university. He married at eighteen, had three children, and then — in the early 1590s — appeared in London as an actor and playwright with no clear account of how he got there. The "lost years" between Stratford and London have generated more scholarly speculation than almost any other biographical mystery in English literature.

By the mid-1590s he was established enough to be both praised and resented by his contemporaries. He was a shareholder in the Globe, a gentleman (he purchased a coat of arms in 1596), and by any measure a successful commercial writer. He was not, in his own lifetime, the transcendent cultural monument he would become. He was a working playwright in a competitive industry, writing approximately two plays a year for a company that needed product.

He retired to Stratford around 1613 and died in 1616. His plays were not collected and published until 1623 — seven years after his death — when his fellow actors Heminges and Condell assembled the First Folio, almost certainly to preserve plays they feared would otherwise be lost. We owe the existence of roughly half of Shakespeare's plays to that act of preservation.

What He Read

Shakespeare was not an original in the modern sense — he almost never invented plots. He borrowed from Holinshed's Chronicles (the histories and Macbeth), from Plutarch (the Roman plays), from Italian novelle (the comedies), and from earlier plays and chronicles (Hamlet). His genius was transformative, not generative: he took existing stories and remade them into something that asked different — and more difficult — questions than the originals had thought to ask.

The 1601 Context

Shakespeare wrote Hamlet in the shadow of a specific political event: the Essex Rebellion of February 1601, in which the Earl of Essex — Elizabeth's former favorite — attempted a coup and was executed. Shakespeare's company had been paid to perform Richard II (a play about the deposition of a king) the night before the rebellion. The connection between theatrical representation and political action had never felt more immediate or more dangerous.

The Text

The Composition of Hamlet

No one knows exactly when Shakespeare wrote Hamlet. The best evidence — a reference to the play in a 1601 document, and the absence of any reference before 1598 — suggests composition between 1599 and 1601. The timing places it at the opening of the Globe, in the middle of Shakespeare's greatest creative decade, and in the immediate shadow of the Essex crisis.

What makes the textual history of Hamlet unusual is that it survives in three substantially different versions — and none of them is simply "right." Each version offers something the others lack, and each reflects a different moment in the play's performance history. Editors have been arguing about which version represents Shakespeare's "real" intentions for four hundred years, with no resolution in sight.

First Quarto — Q1 Title page · 1603 · British Library
The First Quarto
1603 · "The Bad Quarto"

Roughly half the length of the other versions, with different wording, different scene order, and a famous variant: "To be, or not to be, I there's the point." Long thought to be a pirated text reconstructed from memory by actors. Now understood as possibly a legitimate early version or touring text. Scholars no longer dismiss it as simply corrupt.

Second Quarto — Q2 Title page · 1604/5 · British Library
The Second Quarto
1604 / 1605 · "The Good Quarto"

Nearly twice as long as Q1, and widely considered the most authoritative version of Shakespeare's own text — possibly set from his manuscript. Contains passages not in the Folio. The longest of the three versions. A full performance of Q2 would run approximately four hours.

First Folio — F1 Title page · 1623 · Folger Shakespeare Library
The First Folio
1623 · Posthumous Collection

Published seven years after Shakespeare's death by his colleagues Heminges and Condell. The Folio Hamlet differs from Q2 in hundreds of small ways and lacks some passages Q2 contains while adding others. It likely reflects a later performance version, revised for the stage. Most modern editions are conflations of Q2 and F1.

What This Means for Reading the Play

The text you read in your edition of Hamlet is an editorial construction — a modern scholar's decision about which version to follow at each point where they diverge. There is no single, authoritative, Shakespeare-approved text. This is not a problem to be solved; it is a condition of the play's existence, and it tells us something important: Hamlet was always a living, changing thing, performed differently in different contexts, never finished. The instability of the text is part of what the play is.

c. 1589
The Ur-Hamlet
Pre-Shakespeare

A play about Hamlet — possibly by Thomas Kyd — is referenced by contemporary writers. It featured a ghost crying "Hamlet, revenge." This play is now lost. Shakespeare may have been revising an existing theatrical property rather than working from scratch.

1598–1599
The Globe Opens; Shakespeare at his Peak
The Globe

The Globe Theatre is built on Bankside. Shakespeare writes Julius Caesar, As You Like It, and — almost certainly — begins work on Hamlet. He is thirty-five years old and at the height of his powers.

Feb. 1601
The Essex Rebellion
Political Crisis

The Earl of Essex attempts a coup against Elizabeth I and is executed. Shakespeare's company performs Richard II — a play about deposing a king — the night before. The connections between theater, politics, and royal succession have never felt more dangerous.

July 1602
First Recorded Reference to Hamlet
Hamlet

The stationer James Roberts enters "A booke called the Revenge of Hamlett Prince Denmarke" in the Stationers' Register — the first definitive record of the play's existence. This establishes that the play was complete and in performance by mid-1602 at the latest.

1603
First Quarto Published
Q1

The first printed version of Hamlet appears — the short, variant text now called Q1 or the "Bad Quarto." Its title page claims it has been performed "in the City of London: and also in the two Universities of Cambridge and Oxford." Hamlet was already traveling.

1604–1605
Second Quarto Published
Q2

The longer, authoritative Q2 is published, claiming to be "Newly imprinted and enlarged to almost as much againe as it was, according to the true and perfect Coppie." This is the version most closely associated with Shakespeare's own manuscript.

1623
First Folio — Shakespeare Collected
First Folio

Seven years after Shakespeare's death, his colleagues Heminges and Condell publish the First Folio — thirty-six plays, including a version of Hamlet that differs from Q2 in hundreds of details. Without this collection, roughly half of Shakespeare's works would have been lost entirely.

This is the world in which Hamlet was written — warm, crowded, ambitious, morally urgent, and deeply anxious about what comes next. The play you are about to read was made here, for these people, in this light.

What follows is a different world entirely.
