SEC · 01

Two Different Tasks, Done in Order

Editing and proofreading are the final stage of the writing process, and the ones most directly connected to the Sentence Structure, Grammar, Punctuation, and Mechanics, and Vocabulary criteria on the grading rubric. Though they are often treated as one thing, they are genuinely different tasks that work best when done separately and in order: editing first, proofreading second. For a research essay about a dystopian film, each stage has an additional layer: checking how your sources are being used and whether they are documented correctly.

Final Stage
Editing and Proofreading
✏ Editing: Style and Clarity

Improving how your sentences are written. Are they clear? Concise? Well constructed? For research essays, editing also means assessing whether each source is working the way it should and whether your own analytical voice is still leading the argument.

🔎 Proofreading: Errors and Mechanics

Catching mechanical mistakes: grammar, spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and MLA formatting. For research essays, this includes verifying that every in text citation is accurate and that every source cited in the essay has a properly formatted Works Cited entry.

SEC · 02

Editing Your Essay

Editing a research essay works at two levels simultaneously: the sentence level, where you ask whether your sentences are clear, precise, and varied, and the source level, where you ask whether your sources are integrated effectively and appropriately. Work through both passes before moving on to proofreading.

Sentence Level Editing
  • Is every sentence clear and direct, with no unnecessary words?
  • Are you using the specific vocabulary of film analysis (shot, frame, composition, mise en scène, diegetic, nondiegetic, cut, match cut, tracking shot, montage)?
  • Are sentences varied in structure, or does every one begin the same way?
  • Is each body paragraph's topic sentence specific enough to tell the reader exactly what the paragraph argues?
  • Do transitions clearly signal the relationship between ideas, both within paragraphs and between them?
  • Is every film, director, character, and critic discussed in the present tense? (Cuarón "frames," not "framed.")
Source Level Editing
  • Is each source, whether a film moment or a secondary source, introduced with a signal phrase naming the director or author?
  • Does your own analysis follow every quote? Both film moments and secondary source passages need to be analyzed, not just dropped in.
  • Are you quoting too heavily? Your analytical voice should lead the argument; sources support it, not the other way around.
  • Are any secondary source quotes too long? For an essay of this length, keep direct quotes to three typed lines or fewer.
  • Does each source actually support the analytical point of its paragraph, or is it loosely related?
  • Did you meet the assignment requirements: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary from three separate secondary sources?
Cinematic Present Tense :: Film Essays

When analyzing a film, always use the present tense. The film exists as an ongoing artifact; its shots play, its characters speak, its imagery accumulates in the present, not the past. This convention applies to the film itself, to its director, and to what critics and scholars argue about it.

Incorrect Correct
Cuarón used long takes to immerse the viewer in the chase. Cuarón uses long takes to immerse the viewer in the chase.
Ofelia descended into the labyrinth and met the Faun. Ofelia descends into the labyrinth and meets the Faun.
Bordwell argued that the green color palette signaled an unreal world. Bordwell argues that the green color palette signals an unreal world.
💬 Vary Your Signal Phrases

A signal phrase introduces a quote, paraphrase, or summary by naming the author and characterizing what they do. Relying on "According to [author]" for every source is repetitive and weakens your analytical voice. Use the options below, all in the present tense, to vary your introductions and characterize each source's stance accurately.

Neutral / Descriptive
  • notes
  • explains
  • observes
  • writes
  • points out
  • describes
  • traces
Analytical / Argumentative
  • argues
  • contends
  • asserts
  • claims
  • maintains
  • insists
  • proposes
Interpretive
  • suggests
  • implies
  • demonstrates
  • examines
  • concludes

  • reveals
Contrasting / Qualifying

SEC · 03

Proofreading Your Essay

Proofreading catches the errors editing leaves behind: grammar, spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and MLA formatting. For a research essay, verifying the MLA documentation is substantial enough to be its own dedicated pass, a citation audit, completed before you check grammar and mechanics. Work through the steps below in order.

📋 The Three Step Citation Audit
Step 1
Match every in text citation to a Works Cited entry

Find every in text citation in your essay, whether the author appears in the signal phrase or in a parenthetical, and confirm that the same author has an entry on your Works Cited page. The rule works in both directions: if a source is cited in the essay, it must have a Works Cited entry; if a source has a Works Cited entry, it must appear at least once in the essay. Your Works Cited should have exactly four entries: the film as primary source and your three secondary sources.

Step 2
Verify that paraphrases and summaries are attributed

Students reliably cite direct quotes but sometimes forget that paraphrases (a source's idea restated in your own words) and summaries (an overview of a source's overall argument) also require attribution. Read through your essay for any sentences clearly based on a source's ideas that do not identify the author either in the signal phrase or in a parenthetical citation, and correct them. Uncited paraphrases and summaries constitute plagiarism.

Step 3
Review the Works Cited page formatting

Confirm the Works Cited page is correctly formatted: alphabetical order by author's last name (films with no individual author at the front of the entry are alphabetized by title, ignoring "A," "An," and "The"); double spaced throughout with no extra space between entries; hanging indent on each entry (first line flush left, all subsequent lines indented half an inch); the words Works Cited centered at the top in regular, not bold, font; and all URLs in plain text with no color or underline.

Film Specific MLA Format Checks

In addition to the standard citation audit, check the following rules specific to film essays before you submit:

  • Timestamps, not page numbers. Parenthetical citations for the film cite timestamps in the format (Director Last Name HH:MM:SS), for example (Cuarón 00:42:18). The timestamp marks the start of the moment you are citing.
  • Use "to" between timestamps for ranges. When you cite a longer scene, use the word "to" rather than a dash: (Cuarón 00:42:18 to 00:44:05). Do not use any kind of dash inside a timestamp range.
  • Director in the signal phrase, timestamp only in the parenthetical. If you have already named the director in your sentence, you may use only the timestamp in the parenthetical: (00:42:18). If the director has not yet been named in the paragraph, include the surname in the parenthetical.
  • Film title in italics. Film titles always go in italics, both in the essay text and on the Works Cited page. Episode titles and short film titles use quotation marks, but feature length film titles do not.
  • Quoting dialogue. Short lines of dialogue are run into your prose inside quotation marks, followed by the timestamp parenthetical: "We can never know about the days to come, but we think about them anyway" (Cuarón 01:35:42). Do not use slash notation; that is a poetry convention.
  • No parenthetical for HTML sources with author in signal phrase. When a secondary source has no page numbers (HTML format) and you have named the author in your signal phrase, there is no parenthetical citation at all. The absence of a citation is not an error; it is the correct format.
Grammar and Mechanics Checklist

After completing the citation audit, do one final read for sentence level errors. The three most serious errors in academic writing are:

  • Sentence fragments: incomplete sentences that lack a subject, a verb, or both.
  • Comma splices: two complete sentences joined only with a comma, with no coordinating conjunction.
  • Run on sentences: two or more complete sentences run together without adequate punctuation or a conjunction.

Check also for consistent use of cinematic present tense throughout, proper capitalization of directors' names, characters' names, and film titles, and correct spelling of names that are easy to misspell (Cuarón, del Toro, Wachowski, Lyra Belacqua, Ofelia, Theo Faron).
