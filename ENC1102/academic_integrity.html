Academic Integrity: Plagiarism and Its Consequences

What it is, what it looks like, and what happens if you're caught

📖 What Is Plagiarism?

Pilfering. Cribbing. Ripping-Off. Purloining. Jacking. Looting.

The word-nerds among you might have already solved this little puzzle: What do the above terms have in common?

Correct! They're all synonyms for stealing — you know, one of those behaviors that's universally frowned upon (except by kleptomaniacs, but that's for a different class).

In an academic setting, stealing takes a very specific form: Your Old Frenemy, Plagiarism. Plagiarism occurs when one uses another's ideas, words, or work — scholarly, creative — without acknowledging (that is, giving credit to) the original creator. It remains among the most consequential "sins" a student can commit.

Exactly what constitutes this type of thievery is often murky, especially for students who, after sleepily logging in to Canvas, are met in their inbox with the comically pretentious, "J'Accuse!"

Professors can rarely resist showing off their expensive educations.

Rather than yet another Pseudo Authority Figure going Full Brimstone on you, let's adopt a different approach: allow a peer to teach you! In the video below, Chelsea Seburn — a recent college graduate who runs her own successful YouTube channel — defines six different kinds of plagiarism:

1. Direct Plagiarism
2. Find and Replace Plagiarism
3. Patchwork Plagiarism
4. Accidental Plagiarism
5. Self-Plagiarism
6. Total Plagiarism

While these types sometimes go by different names, they're the most commonly recognized forms that can get you into academic trouble.

🗂️ The Six Types at a Glance — Keep This Open During the Quiz

Use the reference cards below to record or review your definitions. You'll be allowed to have this page open during the quiz — so use this section as your cheat sheet. Fill in each definition in your own words, not Chelsea's.

Type 1

Direct Plagiarism

Copying someone else's work word-for-word and presenting it as your own, without quotation marks or any attribution.

Key marker: Exact wording taken directly from a source, with no citation and no quotation marks.
Type 2

Find and Replace Plagiarism

Swapping out individual words for synonyms while keeping the original sentence structure, then presenting it as original writing.

Key marker: Different words, same ideas and structure as the source — and no citation.
Type 3

Patchwork Plagiarism

Stitching together passages or ideas from several different sources to build a paper, without properly crediting any of them.

Key marker: Multiple uncited sources combined to create a "new" piece of writing.
Type 4

Accidental Plagiarism

Unintentionally failing to cite sources correctly — through poor note-taking, forgetting where an idea came from, or misunderstanding citation rules.

Key marker: The intent was not to steal — but the result is the same. Good intentions don't cancel the violation.
Type 5

Self-Plagiarism

Submitting your own previously submitted work — from a different class or a prior semester — as if it were new, original writing for the current assignment.

Key marker: Even if the work is yours, reusing it without permission is a violation. This is why you can't re-use your first essay for this one.
Type 6

Total Plagiarism

Submitting someone else's complete work — an entire paper or essay — as if it were your own. The most severe form.

Key marker: The entire piece came from somewhere else. This includes buying essays online or submitting AI-generated work as your own.
⚖️ What Are the Consequences of Plagiarism?

Once there was a dour French "philosopher" (whatever that means). After enduring the early death of his father, a life-long medical condition, strabismus exotropia, two world wars (including nine months imprisoned in a Nazi work camp), and a very sassy philosopher-girlfriend, this man (understandably, one must concede) entered into middle age as quite the pessimist. In his laughingly-awful "play" titled No Exit, this voluble, incessant thinker (named Jean-Paul Sartre) forces one of his enslaved "characters" to utter a phrase that has since become a near-mantra for a certain flavor of tiresome misanthrope: "Hell is other people."

Sure.

What's demonstrably more certain is the suffering students can expect should they find themselves accused of plagiarism: The Hell of Administrative Bureaucracies.

If students find themselves accused of plagiarism, there are generally two levels of policy-bureaucracy they'll face: Institutional and Instructional.

Institutional Bureaucracy

Valencia College has established its own broad policies to govern acts of academic dishonesty. The second paragraph of the college's official policy establishes that plagiarism violates college policy. The third section identifies the person who will ultimately decide your specific consequences if found guilty: your instructor.

You can read the full college policy at Valencia Policy 6Hx28:8-11 ↗.

Instructional Bureaucracy

College instructors are afforded broad autonomy over the specific rules governing their classes. The consequences for plagiarism can therefore vary from instructor to instructor. Below are examples — starting with our course's policy. Note that what applies in one classroom may not apply in another.

Our Course's Academic Integrity Policy (from the Syllabus)

Valencia College is dedicated to maintaining an academic environment consistent with high standards including a shared commitment to transparency, integrity, and creating the right conditions for learning. All work is expected to reflect individual knowledge, thought, research, effort, and self-expression — or to properly cite the origin of the work.

Original Work: All submitted work must be your own original effort unless explicitly stated otherwise.
Citing Sources: Whenever you use ideas, words, or intellectual property from external sources, cite them using MLA documentation.
Consequences: Violations may result in a zero for the assignment or having to re-do the assignment, in whole or in part. Students have 7 days to respond to any allegation. Violations are reported per Valencia Policy 6Hx28:8-11 ↗.

For our class, the "punishment" is limited to just the specific assignment for which plagiarism was determined — with evidence — to have taken place.

This is by no means always the case. Below is an example policy from another instructor at the college — note the highlighted sentence:

Another Instructor's Policy — Note the Highlighted Sentence

All work submitted by students is expected to be the result of the students' individual thoughts, research, and self-expression. Whenever a student uses ideas, wording, or organization from another source, the source shall be appropriately acknowledged. If a student is caught submitting plagiarized work, a first offense will result in a zero score on the assignment; a second offense will result in a class grade of F.

In other words, being found culpable for plagiarizing could result in you failing an entire class. Yikes!

😬 It Gets Worse

Depending on the situation and the desires of the professor, the consequences can actually be more severe. Below are the general steps that can be taken when someone is accused of plagiarism. Pay special attention to how each option begins: "The professor may choose to." This language explicitly merges Institutional and Instructional Bureaucratic policies in order to determine your academic fate.

The accused plagiarist is presented with three possible futures:

Option 1

Academic Sanction

"Sanction" means "punishment." Here, the student's punishment is limited to the instructional level: failing an assignment, having to redo it, or even failing the whole class. This is the least severe of the three options.

Option 2

Disciplinary Sanction

An instructor can report the incident to the institution as a violation of the Student Code of Conduct. The accused undergoes an institutional-level process resembling a "trial," conducted by college employees and other students. Consequences can extend well beyond the classroom.

Option 3

Academic and Disciplinary Sanctions

This third option combines Options 1 and 2: the student faces both the instructional-level punishment and the institutional-level disciplinary proceedings. This would be the most severe outcome of the three.

Valencia College's Student Rights & Responsibilities Team

While the details of that bureaucratic nightmare are beyond the scope of this lesson (aspiring lawyers can read all about it in Policy 6Hx28:8–03 in the Student Code of Conduct), it's helpful to know about one component of the college's administrative corps: The Student Rights and Responsibilities Team.

This team, along with the Student Conduct Committee (a separate team of Valencia faculty, students, and staff who function somewhat like a jury in a disciplinary hearing), ensures that students' due process rights are upheld but also that any violation of college policy is punished appropriately.

Valencia's philosophical approach tends to emphasize "restorative justice," which generally means focusing on helping students learn from their mistakes rather than on dispensing harsh punishments. Nevertheless, if you ever find an e-mail from this team in your inbox, you should prepare yourself accordingly.

Documents. So-Called Trials. Zeros on Assignments. Policies. Teams. Failing courses. Committees. Sanctions.

This is the possible hell awaiting the poor plagiarist.
