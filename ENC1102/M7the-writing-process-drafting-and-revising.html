Stage Three · Part One

Drafting Your Essay

You have done the hard thinking. You have a poem, an angle, a thesis, an outline, and your sources gathered. Drafting is the stage where you translate that preparation into continuous prose — paragraphs that flow from one to the next and build a single, coherent argument. It is worth remembering that a first draft is not a finished essay. It is a working document. Your primary goal in drafting is to get the ideas down in the right order. Worrying too much about sentence-level perfection at this stage slows you down; revision is what the next pass is for.

For this essay, your drafting stage includes one task you deferred from your outline: filling in the secondary source sections of each body paragraph — items ④ and ⑤. This is the stage where you return to your three sources and decide exactly how to integrate each one at the most effective point in your argument.

Three Required Types of Source Integration

Your three secondary sources must appear as three different types of integration: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary. Review the MLA Documentation pages from this module — especially the new How to Write a Summary page — if you need a refresher on how to write a summary or paraphrase, or how to format parenthetical citations for PDF vs. HTML sources.

Citing the Poem: Line Numbers

When you quote from your poem, MLA format requires line numbers in the parenthetical citation — not page numbers. On your first citation, spell out the word: (lines 3–4). On all subsequent citations, use the number alone: (7–8) or (line 11). Short quotations of three or fewer lines from the poem are run into your prose with a forward slash ( / ) marking each line break. Four or more lines require a block quote. See the MLA Documentation: Quoting from a Poem handout for full guidance.

From Watching to Listening: Imagery and the Arrival of Love in Sylvia Plath's "Morning Song"

● Introduction
① Creative Opening
Opens with an observation about the poem's unusual emotional logic — neither apology nor defense
② Poem & Author Introduced
Title, author, year, and collection identified; historical context established
③ Thesis Statement
Last sentence; states exactly what the two image registers do and what argument they support

In 1961, Sylvia Plath wrote a poem about new motherhood that refused every sentiment the occasion was supposed to require. "Morning Song," published posthumously in Ariel (1965), does not begin with tenderness. It begins with a mechanical simile — the newborn compared to a wound-up watch — and it does not soften quickly. Across six tercets, the poem traces a speaker who initially views her infant with the detachment of a museum visitor and who does not, even by the poem's end, declare uncomplicated love. What she does instead is listen. Plath divides the poem between two distinct image registers: the first half is dominated by cold, visual, object-centered imagery that places the speaker at a distance from her child, and the second by fragile, intimate auditory imagery that slowly draws her back. In "Morning Song," Plath uses this contrast between the mechanical imagery of looking and the vulnerable imagery of listening to argue that maternal love is not an instinct that arrives with birth but a capacity that develops incrementally, through the act of paying attention.

The poem's first three stanzas are dominated by visual imagery that positions the newborn as object rather than person and the speaker as observer rather than mother — a register of deliberate emotional distance. The opening simile establishes the tone immediately: "Love set you going like a fat gold watch" (line 1). The baby has been "set going" as a watch is wound, not held and welcomed into being. "Fat gold" has the quality of something valuable but impersonal — self-contained, ticking away independent of anyone's care. The second stanza extends the cold, visual language: the parents stand "blankly as walls" around a "new statue" in a "drafty museum," which converts the birth scene into something institutional, still, and cold. But the stanza's most striking move comes in the poem's turn away from any maternal feeling at all: "I'm no more your mother / Than the cloud that distils a mirror to reflect its own slow / Effacement at the wind's hand" (7–9). The image is visual and almost aggressively abstract — a cloud reflected in rain that dissolves its own image, leaving nothing. The speaker is not uncertain about her feelings; she denies the relationship entirely, comparing herself to something already in the process of disappearing. Scholar Allison Barrett describes the opening half of the poem as a scene in which "an uncomfortable current of air flows between mother and baby: the baby is something to be looked at, but not held" (68). Barrett's reading clarifies what Plath builds across these first nine lines: the visual imagery of watch, statue, and dissolving cloud is not accidental coldness but a precise record of the speaker's inner state — a distance the poem will spend its second half working to cross.

The poem's emotional turn arrives in stanza four, when the speaker stops looking at the baby and begins listening to it — and the imagery shifts accordingly, from visual to auditory, from cold to fragile, from mechanism to breath. "All night your moth-breath / Flickers among the flat pink roses. I wake to listen: / A far sea moves in my ear" (10–12). The "moth-breath" image marks the poem's first act of genuinely intimate attention: the mother lies awake in the dark, straining to hear the infant's breathing. "Moth-breath" carries the full weight of fragility — moths are nearly weightless, visible only at the edge of light — but "A far sea moves in my ear" converts that fragility into something vast. She hears in this barely audible breathing something as deep and continuous as the ocean. The shift from looking to listening is not merely sensory; it is the first voluntary act of attention the speaker has directed toward the child. When the cry comes, she responds bodily: "One cry, and I stumble from bed, cow-heavy and / floral / In my Victorian nightgown" (13–14). The visual image of "cow-heavy" is deliberately unglamorous — she is animal, slow, not the serene mother of convention — but she has stumbled toward the baby, summoned not by sight but by sound. The auditory has become the means of connection where the visual could not. Critic Lisa Katz argues that what rescues "Morning Song" from becoming a poem of maternal grief is precisely this turn toward sound: the speaker does not bond with her child through instinct or some essentialized femininity but constructs her own bond through the shared territory of voice and listening, making use of sound and language rather than an inherited maternal feeling (117). Katz's reading illuminates why the poem's closing image lands with such force: "The clear vowels rise like balloons" (19). Sound has become sight — the baby's vowels lift into the air, light and celebratory — and the image's joy stands in stark contrast to the cold mechanism of the opening line. In his broader reading of Plath's poetics in "Precarious Verse," Erwin Montgomery argues that Plath systematically deflates her subjects, reducing persons and things to bare phenomena and refusing any sentimental adornment — a leveling move that, in "Morning Song," clears away the expected language of maternal devotion so that something more fragile and honest can take its place. Montgomery's framing clarifies the stakes of the poem's imagery: the balloons are not a resolution. They are a beginning — the sound of a child's voice heard, finally, as beautiful.

● Conclusion
① Creative Opening
Does not begin with "In conclusion"; returns to the poem's last word as a frame
② Poem Referenced
Brief reference to close the essay's frame (grey highlight)
③ Thesis Restated
In new words; does not repeat the introduction's phrasing
④ Broader Significance
Extends the argument beyond the poem to a larger insight
⑤ Memorable Closing Sentence
Leaves the reader with something to hold onto

By the poem's last line, the baby's "clear vowels rise like balloons" (19) — and by this point, Plath has made that rising feel earned. Nothing has been resolved neatly. The speaker has not declared that she loves her child or announced that the distance she felt in the opening stanzas has closed. What has changed is her mode of attention: she is no longer regarding the baby as a mechanism or a statue; she is listening to it, stumbling toward it in the dark, hearing the sea in its breathing and joy in its vowels. Plath's two image registers — the cold, mechanical visual imagery of the poem's first half and the fragile, intimate auditory imagery of its second — trace this shift with precise economy. The poem asks nothing more of love than this: not the overwhelming instinct we expect it to be, but the practice of attending carefully enough to hear what is small and fragile as something immense. "Morning Song" argues that love, at its beginning, is mostly the willingness to wake up and listen.

✎ Works Cited Format Notes

Alphabetical order: Entries are arranged by the author's last name. "A," "An," and "The" at the start of a title are skipped when alphabetizing.

Hanging indent: The second and subsequent lines of each entry are indented half an inch — the opposite of a paragraph indent.

Citing a poem: For a poem, MLA 9 lists the author, poem title in quotation marks, collection title in italics, publisher, and year. In-text citations use line numbers, not page numbers: (lines 3–4) on first use, (7–8) thereafter.

Database entries: When a source was accessed through an academic database, add the database name (italicized) and the URL or stable link as a second container at the end of the entry.

PDF vs. HTML sources: If a source is a PDF or print document with page numbers, include the page number in the parenthetical citation: (422). If a source is an HTML webpage with no page numbers, name the author in the signal phrase — and then use no parenthetical at all.

Four entries total: Your primary source (the poem) plus all three secondary sources must appear on the Works Cited.

Stage Three · Part Two

Revising Your Draft

Revising is not proofreading. Proofreading — fixing spelling, grammar, and formatting — comes in Stage Four. Revision is re-thinking and re-shaping: reading your draft with fresh eyes to ask whether the argument holds, whether the evidence supports it, and whether every paragraph does what you said it would do. The four revision questions below give you a framework for that re-reading.

1
Argument & Thesis
  • Does your thesis make a specific, arguable claim — not just a summary of what the poem is about?
  • Does every body paragraph advance that claim, or does any paragraph drift into description without analysis?
  • Does your conclusion restate the thesis in new words and add a broader reflection?
2
Primary Source Evidence
  • Does each body paragraph contain 2–3 brief, direct quotes from the poem?
  • Are all poem quotes introduced with a signal phrase before the quote begins?
  • Is each quote followed by your own analysis — not just a re-statement of what it says?
  • Are line numbers cited correctly: "lines" on first use, numbers only thereafter?
3
Secondary Source Integration
  • Have you used all three required types: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary?
  • Is the PDF/database source cited with a page number in parentheses?
  • Are both HTML sources cited correctly — author named in signal phrase, no parenthetical at all?
  • After each secondary source, does your analysis explain how it connects to your argument?
4
Structure & Flow
  • Does your introduction end with the thesis?
  • Does each body paragraph open with a topic sentence that names the paragraph's specific focus?
  • Does each body paragraph close with a sentence that ties its evidence back to the thesis?
  • Does your conclusion avoid introducing new quotes or new evidence?
