How to Use This Practice

Each poem has four locked steps. Work through Step 1 before unlocking Step 2, and so on. Don't skip ahead — the steps build on each other. Read the poem carefully, attempt each task on your own first, then reveal the model answer.

Your goal isn't to match the model answer word-for-word. It's to practice the habit of reading: noticing specific language, naming what it does, and explaining why it matters. The model shows you one strong reading — yours may differ and still be valid.

Poem One

Sonnet 18 ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?")

William Shakespeare, 1609
Add
portrait
William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright, poet, and actor widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language. His 154 sonnets, published in 1609, are fourteen-line poems written in iambic pentameter exploring themes of love, beauty, time, and immortality. Sonnet 18 — "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" — is perhaps the most famous poem in the English language. Notice how Shakespeare builds an extended comparison in the opening lines, then systematically dismantles it before arriving at his central claim.

Sonnet 18 — William Shakespeare (1609)
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate. Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, And summer's lease hath all too short a date. Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines, And often is his gold complexion dimmed; And every fair from fair sometime declines, By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimmed. But thy eternal summer shall not fade, Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow'st, Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade, When in eternal lines to Time thou grow'st. So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.
Public domain. First published in Shakespeare's Sonnets, 1609.
Annotated Poem — Figurative Language Identified
Poem Text
Figurative Language
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate. Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, And summer's lease hath all too short a date.
Simile (extended)
The entire poem springs from this opening question. "Compare thee to a summer's day" is the explicit simile — you are like a summer's day. Lines 2–12 then test and complicate that comparison.
Personification
"Rough winds" have the human quality of roughness (attitude/mood). The buds are called "darlings" — a term of endearment, as if the winds are bullying beloved children.
Metaphor
"Summer's lease" — summer is a tenant renting time. A lease has an expiration date. Summer doesn't own the season; it only borrows it.
Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines, And often is his gold complexion dimmed; And every fair from fair sometime declines, By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimmed.
Metaphor
"The eye of heaven" = the sun. This metaphor gives the sky a face. It also implies judgment — an eye watches. "His gold complexion" continues the personification: the sun has a face, a skin tone, a mood.
Personification
"Nature's changing course" is given agency — nature is a force that acts, that trims and untrimmed things. Nature is a gardener or tailor cutting back beauty.
But thy eternal summer shall not fade, Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow'st, Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade, When in eternal lines to Time thou grow'st.
Metaphor
"Thy eternal summer" — the beloved's beauty is summer, but unlike the real season, theirs will last. The metaphor inverts the opening simile: the beloved isn't just like summer; they contain an immortal version of it.
Personification
"Death brag" — Death is a boastful figure who claims souls as trophies. But the poem's speaker says Death won't get to brag here. Death is given a personality: vain, competitive, gloating.
Metaphor
"Eternal lines to Time" = the poem itself. The sonnet is lines that grow into time — immortalizing the beloved the way a tree grows its rings into history.
So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.
Metaphor
"This gives life to thee" — the poem is not just words; it is a living force. The poem gives life. The metaphor completes the argument: writing = immortality.
2
Step 2 — Analyze the Devices
What is each comparison doing? What are the connotations?
🔒
Your task

For each figurative phrase below, think carefully: what two things are being compared? What qualities does the comparison import? What would be lost if Shakespeare had used plain language instead? Write your thoughts, then reveal each card.

"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
Simile
What qualities does "a summer's day" carry for an Elizabethan reader? Why does Shakespeare immediately admit this comparison is imperfect?
Summer = warmth, beauty, abundance, peak life. But Shakespeare immediately says "thou art more lovely" — the simile is set up only to be rejected. The beloved exceeds summer, which can be too hot, too short, and unreliable. By opening with a comparison the poem then dismantles, Shakespeare argues that conventional comparisons are not good enough for this person.
"summer's lease hath all too short a date"
Metaphor
Summer is a tenant, not an owner. What does calling it a "lease" imply about how nature operates — and what beauty cannot hold onto?
A lease is a temporary agreement. The tenant (summer) must leave when the contract expires. Beauty in the natural world is on borrowed time — it doesn't own itself. The legal vocabulary ("lease," "possession" in line 10) runs throughout the poem, suggesting that ownership of beauty is the central problem Shakespeare is solving through the poem itself.
"the eye of heaven"
Metaphor
The sun is called an "eye." What does this personify about the sky — and what does it imply about the sun's behavior when it gets "too hot" or "dimmed"?
An eye watches, observes, and judges. "Heaven" looking down gives the universe moral attention — the sky sees us. But this eye is also unreliable: it sometimes shines too hot (glaring, oppressive) and sometimes dims (hidden by clouds). The beloved, by contrast, will not be dimmed. The contrast positions the beloved as more constant than even the sun.
"Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade"
Personification
Death is given a personality here. What kind of person is Death in this image — and why does it matter that Shakespeare imagines Death as specifically boastful?
Death is competitive and vain — a braggart who collects souls as trophies. By giving Death this personality, Shakespeare turns the poem's final argument into a contest that the poem wins. Death won't get to boast about this beloved because the poem preserves them. The personification makes death into a rival the poet can actually defeat — which is the poem's central claim.
"thy eternal summer"
Metaphor
Earlier, being compared to "a summer's day" was inadequate. Now Shakespeare gives the beloved "thy eternal summer." How does returning to the summer metaphor, but modified, change its meaning?
"Eternal" is what natural summer lacks. The regular summer has a short lease; the beloved's version does not. By adding "eternal" to the metaphor, Shakespeare solves the problem raised in the first quatrain. The beloved isn't like an ordinary summer day — they have their own summer that lives forever in the poem. The device completes a circular structure: simile (introduced) → dismantled → rebuilt as metaphor.

Step 3 — Connections & Patterns
What do the figurative devices have in common? What argument do they build together?
🔒
Your task

Look back at all the figurative language you identified. You'll notice the poem has two distinct phases: in the first eight lines, Shakespeare builds comparisons to break them down. In the final six lines, he asserts the beloved's permanence. How do the figurative devices support this structure? What pattern emerges? Think it through, then reveal the model.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections
Lines 1–8: Comparison → Failure

The opening simile ("compare thee to a summer's day") and its follow-up metaphors ("summer's lease," "eye of heaven") all share one quality: they describe things that are unreliable, temporary, or imperfect. Summer is too short. The sun is too hot or too dim. Nature is "untrimmed." Every figurative device in the first eight lines is introduced to show why it's not good enough. This is Shakespeare's rhetorical setup.

Lines 9–14: Permanence Claimed

"Thy eternal summer," "eternal lines to Time," "this gives life to thee." The second half flips the devices: now the metaphors assert permanence rather than failure. Death is personified but defeated. The poem itself becomes a metaphor for immortality. Each device in the final six lines answers a problem raised in the first eight.

The Central Pattern: Legal Language + Nature

Notice that Shakespeare weaves legal vocabulary throughout the figurative language: "lease," "date," "lose possession," "ow'st." Nature is a tenant who must leave. But the poem argues that art is different — it owns time. The figurative language moves from nature's impermanence to art's permanence, and legal metaphors track that movement. The poem argues, through its own figurative devices, that the act of writing defeats death.

Step 4 — Interpretation
What is Shakespeare arguing? What does the figurative language reveal about the poem's purpose?
🔒
Your task

You've worked through the poem's figurative devices, their connotations, and their patterns. Now put it all together: what is this sonnet doing? What argument does Shakespeare make through the figurative language — not just about the beloved, but about poetry itself? Draft your interpretation, then reveal the model.

Model Interpretation
One Strong Reading

In Sonnet 18, Shakespeare uses a progression of figurative language — simile, metaphor, and personification — to build and then solve a philosophical problem: how do we preserve what is beautiful when time destroys everything? The poem opens with a simile that it immediately undermines ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" — but summer is too short, too unreliable). Every metaphor in the first eight lines describes something that fails: summer holds its lease briefly; the sun is moody and inconsistent; nature's beauty is "untrimmed" by accident and time. Then the poem pivots. "But thy eternal summer shall not fade" reclaims the metaphor, and "death brag" personifies the poem's greatest enemy — only to defeat it. The argument is not just about a beloved person; it is about what poetry can do. The final couplet makes the claim explicit: "this gives life to thee." Figurative language is not decoration in this sonnet — it is the mechanism of the argument. The poem argues, through its very use of metaphor, that language outlasts everything nature can offer.

A Note on Interpretation

Some readers focus more on the beloved as subject; others on Shakespeare as the poem's real hero (saving his own art from oblivion). Both are defensible readings. What matters is that you ground your interpretation in the specific figurative language — the devices are the evidence.

Poem Two

Photosynthesis

Ashley M. Jones, from REPARATIONS NOW! (Hub City Press, 2021)
Add
portrait
Ashley M. Jones (b. 1990) is an Alabama poet and the first Black poet laureate of Alabama (2022–present). She grew up in Birmingham — a city whose history is inseparable from the Civil Rights Movement — and her work draws on family, faith, the natural world, and the ongoing reckoning with racial injustice in the American South. Her collection REPARATIONS NOW! (Hub City Press, 2021) explores what is owed and what has been taken. "Photosynthesis" uses the science of plant growth — how a plant converts sunlight and soil into life — as an extended metaphor for her father's labor, his freedom, and the meaning of owning the ground you work.

Photosynthesis — Ashley M. Jones
When I was young, my father taught us how dirt made way for food, how to turn over soil so it would hold a seed, an infant bud, how the dark could nurse it until it broke its green arms out to touch the sun. In his garden, even problems become energy, beauty — my father has ended many work days in the backyard, worries of the firehouse dropping like grain, my father wrist-deep in soil. I am convinced the earth speaks back to him as he feeds it — it is a conversational labor, gardening. The seeds tell him what they will be, the soil tells seeds how to grow, my father speaks sun and water into the earth, we hear him, each harvest, his heartbeat sweet, like fruit. What you call waste, I call power. What you call work I make beautiful again. This is the work we have always known — pulling food and flowers from a pile of earth. The difference, now: my father is not a slave, not a sharecropper. This land is his and so is this garden, so is this work. The difference is that he owns this labor.
From REPARATIONS NOW! by Ashley M. Jones. Copyright © 2021 Ashley M. Jones. Published by Hub City Press. Used with permission. View at Academy of American Poets.
Annotated Poem — Figurative Language Identified
Poem Text
Figurative Language
When I was young, my father taught us how dirt made way for food, how to turn over soil so it would hold a seed, an infant bud, how the dark could nurse it until it broke its green arms out to touch the sun.
Metaphor
"An infant bud" — the seed/bud is compared to an infant child. This links the garden immediately to family, birth, and nurturing. The plant is a baby; the soil is a parent.
Personification (×2)
"The dark could nurse it" — darkness is a nursing mother, a caregiver that sustains life in the soil before the plant reaches light. Then "broke its green arms out to touch the sun" — the plant reaches up like a child stretching toward a parent. It has arms; it touches.
In his garden, even problems become energy, beauty — my father has ended many work days in the backyard, worries of the firehouse dropping like grain, my father wrist-deep in soil.
Metaphor
"Problems become energy" — this is the poem's central metaphor made explicit: photosynthesis converts one thing (light, CO₂) into another (sugar, growth). Here, problems convert into energy. The father's garden does to stress what plants do to sunlight.
Simile
"Worries of the firehouse dropping like grain" — anxieties from the father's job as a firefighter fall away from him the way grain falls. The simile gives the worries physical weight and mass, then lets them drop. The labor of the garden releases what the labor of firefighting holds.
I am convinced the earth speaks back to him as he feeds it — it is a conversational labor, gardening. The seeds tell him what they will be, the soil tells seeds how to grow, my father speaks sun and water into the earth, we hear him, each harvest, his heartbeat sweet, like fruit.
Personification (extended)
The earth "speaks back," the seeds "tell him," the soil "tells seeds." Jones creates an entire conversation between her father and the garden. Three non-human things are given voice in three lines. The garden is not just a place — it is a community of speaking beings.
Simile
"His heartbeat sweet, like fruit" — the father's heartbeat is compared to fruit: ripe, nourishing, the result of patient labor. The simile collapses the distinction between gardener and garden. He has become what he grows.
What you call waste, I call power. What you call work I make beautiful again. This is the work we have always known — pulling food and flowers from a pile of earth. The difference, now: my father is not a slave, not a sharecropper. This land is his and so is this garden, so is this work. The difference is that he owns this labor.
Metaphor (implied)
"What you call waste, I call power" — the metaphor redefines what soil/compost/labor mean. "Waste" and "power" are competing metaphors for the same material. What outsiders see as refuse or degraded labor, Jones reclaims as generative force. This is photosynthesis as politics: transformation is in who gets to name what something is.

Step 2 — Analyze the Devices
What is each comparison doing? What does it add that plain language could not?
🔒
Your task

This poem's figurative language clusters around a single process — photosynthesis — but uses that process to talk about family, history, and freedom. For each device below, think about: what two things are being linked? What does that connection reveal about what Jones is saying? Attempt your own analysis, then reveal.

"an infant bud"
Metaphor
A plant bud is compared to an infant. What does this import into the garden — and how does it set up the poem's later claims about labor and inheritance?
An infant is helpless, dependent, precious, and full of future. By calling the bud an infant, Jones frames the garden as a nursery — a place where new life is tended. This sets up the entire poem's logic: the father's gardening is not just work, it is parenting. The garden is family. Later, when Jones connects Black labor to the soil's history, the "infant bud" reminds us that all of this began with someone learning to nurture something into growth.
"the dark could nurse it"
Personification
Darkness is usually associated with danger or death. Here it nurses. How does Jones transform the connotation of darkness — and what does this say about the unseen labor that makes growth possible?
To nurse is to feed, protect, and sustain something vulnerable. Jones gives darkness a nurturing personality, reversing its typical associations. In the soil, darkness is where germination happens — where a seed becomes something. This revaluation of darkness runs throughout the poem: the "waste" others dismiss becomes power; the dark earth is the source, not the absence, of life. Read against the poem's racial history, this is also a reclamation of Blackness itself as a generative, nurturing force.
"worries of the firehouse dropping like grain"
Simile
Work stress falls "like grain." What is grain — and what happens to grain when it falls? What does comparing worry to grain say about the relationship between one kind of labor and another?
Grain falls to be planted or to be ground. It is a harvest product — something produced by labor that then becomes nourishment. "Worries dropping like grain" means: the stress doesn't vanish, it becomes something useful. The anxiety of firefighting (a dangerous, exhausting job) is transformed by the garden into potential growth, the way grain becomes bread. The simile connects two forms of labor: the institutional danger of the firehouse and the personal sanctuary of the backyard — and shows the garden absorbing what the job releases.
"the earth speaks back to him… The seeds tell him what they will be"
Personification
Earth speaks; seeds tell; soil instructs. Jones creates a dialogue between her father and the garden. What kind of relationship does personification establish — and what does "conversational labor" mean when both parties can speak?
Conversation requires two participants with something to say. By giving the earth and seeds voices, Jones transforms her father's gardening from solitary toil into genuine relationship. This matters historically: enslaved and sharecropped labor was not conversational — it was extraction, a one-way taking. Jones's father's garden is the opposite: a reciprocal exchange where soil and gardener each give and receive. The personification makes this argument without stating it directly. It shows, rather than tells, what freedom in labor looks like.
"his heartbeat sweet, like fruit"
Simile
A heartbeat is sound and rhythm. Fruit is taste and growth. How does comparing a heartbeat to fruit collapse the boundary between the man and his garden?
Fruit is the final product of patient cultivation — the harvest. A heartbeat is the sign of life, heard at its most intimate. By calling his heartbeat "sweet, like fruit," Jones says: the harvest that matters most is the man himself. He is not separate from his garden; he is its highest yield. The simile also returns to the opening metaphor of photosynthesis — the plant takes in light and soil and produces sweetness. So does the father. The comparison fuses the biological process with the human one.

Step 3 — Connections & Patterns
How do the figurative devices build on each other? What is the poem's central argument?

Your task

This poem uses figurative language across three distinct registers: the biological (photosynthesis, plant growth), the familial (infant, nursing, heartbeat), and the historical (slavery, sharecropping, ownership). Look at the figurative devices you've analyzed. How do they move between these registers? What pattern do you see? Then reveal the model.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections
Personification: Voice and Relationship

The darkness nurses, the earth speaks back, the seeds tell, the soil instructs. Every personification in the poem creates a relationship of mutual care — a back-and-forth between a living thing and a person. These devices collectively argue that gardening is not just labor; it is communion. The garden is a community that speaks and listens.

Metaphor: Transformation

"An infant bud," "problems become energy," "what you call waste, I call power." Each metaphor involves transformation — one thing becoming another. This mirrors photosynthesis itself (the poem's central extended metaphor): a plant converts raw materials into life. Jones's figurative language enacts the process it describes.

The Pattern: Conversion and Ownership

Every figurative device in the poem performs a conversion: darkness → nurture, worries → grain, problems → energy, waste → power, heartbeat → fruit. These conversions are the poem's real subject — and they are answered in the final stanza's plain speech: "The difference is that he owns this labor." The figurative language shows what ownership makes possible. When you own your labor, your worries can become grain, your garden can speak back, your heartbeat can ripen. Shakespeare converted beauty into immortality through art; Jones converts labor — historically stolen — into beauty through ownership. The poems are in conversation with each other about what can be preserved and who gets to preserve it.

Model Interpretation
One Strong Reading

In "Photosynthesis," Ashley M. Jones uses a cascade of figurative language — personification of soil and seed, metaphors of conversion, similes linking labor to nature — to argue that the meaning of her father's gardening is inseparable from the history of Black labor in America. The poem opens with biological intimacy: darkness nurses, buds are infants, plants reach out arms. This language immediately establishes the garden as a place of family and care, not just productivity. The central extended metaphor — photosynthesis — does exactly what the process it describes does: it converts one thing into another. Problems become energy; worries drop like grain; even a heartbeat ripens like fruit. But the poem earns its final lines through this figurative groundwork. When Jones writes "my father is not a slave, not a sharecropper. This land is his," the plainness of that declaration lands with full weight because we have just watched, through metaphor and personification, what ownership of labor actually looks like: it looks like a garden that speaks back to you. The figurative language in "Photosynthesis" is not ornamental. It is the poem's argument — that freedom is the condition under which conversion becomes possible, and that beauty is what happens when labor finally belongs to the person doing it.

Connecting the Two Poems

Both Shakespeare and Jones use figurative language to argue for a kind of immortality or preservation. Shakespeare preserves a beloved through the metaphor of the poem as eternal life. Jones preserves her father's labor — and all the labor that preceded it — through a garden that speaks, grows, and is finally owned. Both poems ask: what survives? The answer, in both cases, is built through figurative language itself.
